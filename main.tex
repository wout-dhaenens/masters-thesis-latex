\chapter{Introduction}
\label{intro}




\section{The imperative for decarbonization in the building sector}
The global energy transition is increasingly focused on the urgent need to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and mitigate climate change.
A critical battleground in this transition is the building sector, which remains a significant contributor to energy use and carbon emissions.
For instance, in Belgium alone, the building sector\footnote{In this context, the building sector definition follows the European climate reporting categories, which encompass direct emissions from fossil fuel combustion in residential and tertiary (commercial/institutional) buildings, as well as energy-related emissions from the agriculture and fishery sectors.} is responsible for approximately 20\% of total carbon dioxide ($CO_2$) emissions in 2022 \cite{EU_Climate_Action_Belgium_2023}.
To meet ambitious climate goals, such as those outlined by the European Union, there is a pressing need to decarbonize the heat supply for buildings.
Under the \textit{European Climate Law}, the EU is legally committed to a progressive decarbonization pathway: a net greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions reduction of at least 55\% by 2030 (compared to 1990 levels), an intermediate target of 90\% by 2040, and the ultimate goal of climate neutrality by 2050 \cite{EU_Climate_Law_2021, EU_2040_Target_2026}.
For the building sector specifically, the revised \textit{Energy Performance of Buildings Directive} (EPBD) mandates that all new buildings must be Zero-Emission Buildings (ZEB) by 2030, with a complete phase-out of fossil-fuel-based heating systems required by 2040 \cite{EPBD_2024}.
Several member states have already translated this directive into binding national or regional legislation.
Flanders phased in a prohibition on new gas connections in stages: large residential subdivisions were excluded from new gas infrastructure from 2021, and since January 2025 the ban applies to all new construction -- residential and non-residential alike -- for which a building permit is requested \cite{VEA_GasBoilerBan_2021}.
This regional measure underscores that the phase-out of fossil-fuel heating is already underway in practice, not merely a long-term policy ambition.
The urgency is further amplified by the long asset lifetimes involved: a gas boiler or gas installation commissioned today will typically remain in service for 15--20 years, meaning that every new fossil-fuel installation effectively locks in carbon emissions well beyond 2040 and risks becoming a stranded asset before the end of its economic life.
The significance of the building sector in this context stems largely from the high carbon intensity of thermal energy demands.
Space heating (SH) and domestic hot water heating (DHW) account for approximately 77,6\% of the total energy consumption in residential buildings in Belgium in 2023 \cite{Eurostat_Energy_Consumption_2023}.
Because much of this demand is currently met through the direct combustion of fossil fuels, such as natural gas and heating oil, the thermal energy share is the primary driver of the sector's operational $CO_2$ emissions.
This transition relies heavily on shifting away from fossil-fuel-based systems, such as gas boilers, toward electrification using renewable energy sources (RES) \cite{EnergyVille_PATHS2050_Residential_2025} \cite{Esen2006}.

\section{The role of heat pumps and challenges in refurbishment}
Heat pump systems (HPSs) have emerged as a key technology for electrifying SH and DHW production\cite{Esen2006}.
Their ability to generate thermal energy efficiently from electricity makes them a cornerstone of sustainable building energy systems.
However, the widespread adoption of heat pumps faces significant economic barriers, particularly in the refurbishment of existing buildings.
In these scenarios, heat pumps often compete with gas boilers, which traditionally have lower investment costs.
Furthermore, older buildings often utilize radiator heating systems requiring high flow temperatures (up to $70^{\circ}C$), unlike modern underfloor heating systems, which are designed for low flow temperatures of $35$--$45^{\circ}C$\cite{Oubenmoh2018}.
Consequently, heat pumps for elevated temperatures are essential for these applications to ensure comfort without extensive renovations.
To increase market penetration and motivate homeowners to switch to low-carbon solutions, HPSs must become more economically competitive. The Flemish government and the heat pump sector have recently established a 
collaborative framework to address these economic barriers and accelerate 
the deployment of HPSs \cite{VEKA2026}.
To shift the market from a 10\% adoption rate toward a 70\% target for 
renovations, the strategy focuses on three primary economic levers.
First, capital barriers are mitigated through substantial direct subsidies, 
where premiums can reach up to €8,000 per installation.
Furthermore, the provision of interest-free or low-interest renovation 
loans allows for the pre-financing of the high upfront costs, aligning 
the investment with long-term energy savings.
Second, a strategic energy tax shift is being implemented to reduce the 
price of electricity relative to fossil fuels, ensuring that HPSs remain 
the most cost-effective solution for daily operation.
Finally, the industry has committed to a "Heat Pump Charter" to enhance 
installation quality and provide standardized technical guidance, ensuring 
that systems are optimized for the specific flow requirements of 
refurbished buildings \cite{VEKA2026}.

\section{The transition to renewable heat supply: challenges and solutions}

The urgent need to reduce dependence on fossil fuels has accelerated the integration of RES, such as PV systems, into the built environment.
This shift is a cornerstone of sustainable development, yet the transition toward a fully renewable heat supply introduces a fundamental challenge: the intermittency of sources like solar and wind.
Unlike fossil fuels, where energy supply is constant and dispatchable, the generation of RES fluctuates based on weather conditions.
This stochastic nature poses a direct risk to grid stability and the security of energy supply.

\subsection{Flexibility through electrification and storage}
To mitigate these fluctuations and ensure reliability, the electrification of heat demand must be supported by intelligent control and storage technologies.
The following components are essential to framing this transition:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Demand Side Management (DSM):} 
        This strategy steers electrical loads to match the availability of renewable generation, optimizing the timing of consumption.
            \item \textbf{Thermal Energy Storage (TES):} 
                By coupling TES units with heat pumps, heat generation can be decoupled from immediate demand. 
                    This provides the necessary flexibility to shift electrical loads to periods when renewable electricity is abundant or cheaper.
                        \item \textbf{Electrical Storage (Batteries):} 
                            Integrating battery systems alongside PV generation further stabilizes the grid by maximizing self-consumption and reducing peak loads on the distribution network.
                            \end{itemize}
                            
                            
                            
                            While the synergy between PV systems, heat pumps, and storage technologies offers a robust path toward a fossil-free future, it introduces significant layers of control complexity.
                            The primary challenge lies in balancing the maximization of energy savings and grid support with the non-negotiable requirement of maintaining user comfort, such as consistent domestic hot water temperatures and a stable indoor climate.
                            
                            
                    



\section{The design gap: From static rules to dynamic optimization}
Despite the clear benefits of hybrid energy systems, current design practices remain a bottleneck.
European normative standards for dimensioning HPSs (such as EN 15450 \cite{EN15450_2007}) typically rely on static rules and single operating points.
These procedures often determine the size of individual components sequentially, neglecting the complex interdependencies between the heat pump, storage sizing, and control strategies.
This static approach can lead to sub-optimal designs.
Furthermore, the optimal size of a system is heavily dependent on its operation; a system designed without considering its control strategy may be oversized and economically inefficient \cite{Dongellini2017}.
Therefore, to achieve a truly optimal and sustainable design, dimensioning and operation must be optimized simultaneously.  By optimizing
the system, investment costs can be reduced by approximately 45\% in comparison to normative standards,
while maintaining security of supply and not increasing electrical energy consumption\cite{Krutzfeldt2021}.



\section{Thesis objective}
This thesis aims to bridge this gap by developing a comprehensive design tool in Python using MILP.
The tool will determine the optimal economic sizing of a hybrid energy system comprising a high-temperature heat pump (HP), PV, battery storage, and TES.
By incorporating detailed modeling of system dynamics and component interdependencies, this work seeks to provide a solution that is not only economically viable but also environmentally sustainable, accelerating the decarbonization of the residential heating sector.

\subsection{Case study application: transitioning M-accent from gas to hybrid electrification}

The practical necessity for advanced optimization is exemplified by the M-accent case study, which currently relies on two 80~kW gas boilers for its primary heat supply. Decarbonizing such a system requires a transition to a hybrid energy architecture. To replace the existing 160~kW fossil-fuel capacity, this thesis proposes a system integrating an HP, PV, Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), and TES.

The integration of these technologies presents a complex sizing problem that exceeds the capabilities of static design standards like EN 15450 \cite{EN15450_2007}. Specifically, the design tool developed in this work addresses the following technical challenges identified in literature:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{HP Performance:} Unlike traditional boilers, the capacity and COP of HPs are highly dependent on source and sink temperatures. Following the methodology of Krützfeldt et al. \cite{Krutzfeldt2021}, the tool utilizes MILP (see \autoref{sub:milp_advantages}) to account for these non-linearities through piecewise linear approximations or regressive modeling based on manufacturer data, such as the Daikin EWYE-CZ.
    
    \item \textbf{Storage Synergies:} The tool optimizes the trade-off between electrochemical (BESS) and thermal (TES) storage. While the BESS facilitates PV self-consumption, TES allows for the decoupling of heat production from demand, which is crucial for managing the peak loads previously handled by the 80~kW boilers \cite{Steen2015, Krien2020oemofsolph}.
    
    \item \textbf{Economic Dispatch:} By utilizing MILP, the tool simultaneously determines the optimal investment (sizing) and the hourly operational strategy (dispatch) to minimize the Total Cost of Ownership (TCO), accounting for fluctuating electricity prices and Belgian climate targets \cite{EU_Climate_Action_Belgium_2023}.
\end{itemize}

Ultimately, this tool serves as a bridge between high-level decarbonization goals and the engineering reality of the M-accent project, providing a robust mathematical framework to ensure that the replacement of gas-fired assets results in both economic and environmental gains.

\section{Integrated building energy systems}
\label{sec:lit_building_systems}
% ============================================================

The progressive electrification of building heating, driven by climate targets and falling equipment costs, has produced a new class of multi-technology configurations that integrate renewable generation, heat production, and energy storage in a single controllable system.
Figure~\ref{fig:smart_energy_system} illustrates the canonical topology: a rooftop PV array generates variable DC electricity converted to AC via an inverter; a battery energy storage system (BESS) absorbs surplus generation or supplements supply during peak-tariff periods; a heat pump (HP) converts electrical energy into heat at a useful supply temperature; and a thermal energy storage (TES) tank decouples the timing of heat production from heat demand.
All four assets share a common AC bus that is also connected to the public electricity grid, enabling both import and export.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.78\textwidth]{images/smart_home_energy_system.jpg}
    \caption{Illustrative concept of an integrated smart building energy system, combining rooftop PV generation, battery storage, a heat pump, thermal storage, and two-way grid interaction.}
    \label{fig:smart_energy_system}
\end{figure}

The following subsections describe each technology class, focusing on the variants most relevant to commercial building applications and the key parameters that govern their techno-economic performance.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Heat pump technologies}
\label{sub:lit_hp_tech}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

A heat pump extracts low-grade thermal energy from a heat source and upgrades it to a useful supply temperature, consuming electrical work in the process.
The thermodynamic efficiency is expressed by the coefficient of performance (COP), defined as the ratio of heat output to electrical input.
Three configurations are relevant at commercial building scale:

\textbf{Air-source heat pumps (ASHP)} use outdoor air as the heat source and are the most widely deployed configuration due to low installation cost and absence of ground works.
Their COP varies with ambient temperature, improving in mild weather and degrading at sub-zero conditions \cite{Ruhnau2019}.
Standard ASHP units supply heating water up to approximately 55\,\textdegree C, which limits compatibility with older radiator circuits designed for higher supply temperatures.

\textbf{Ground-source heat pumps (GSHP)} extract heat from the ground or groundwater via borehole or horizontal collector fields.
The comparatively stable temperature of the ground throughout the year yields a higher and more consistent annual COP than air-source systems.
Installation costs are substantially higher and site suitability depends on soil conditions and available land area, which can be a constraint in dense commercial contexts.

\textbf{High-temperature heat pumps (HTHP)} are a specialised class engineered to deliver supply temperatures in the range of 65--90\,\textdegree C, making them compatible with existing hydronic circuits designed for high supply temperatures without requiring replacement of distribution infrastructure \cite{Krutzfeldt2021}.
This is particularly relevant for commercial building retrofits, where disrupting the existing heating network carries disproportionate cost and operational risk.
The elevated temperature lift is achieved at the cost of a lower COP compared with conventional ASHP units operating under identical ambient conditions.
Unit performance is characterised by manufacturer test data at defined source and sink temperatures, from which an empirical efficiency factor can be derived for use in analytical COP models \cite{Rogeau2024HPPL}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Thermal energy storage technologies}
\label{sub:lit_tes_overview}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Thermal energy storage decouples the temporal profile of heat production from the building's heat demand, enabling the HP to operate preferentially during low-electricity-price periods and to absorb surplus PV generation as heat.
Three technology classes are relevant at building scale: sensible heat storage (water tanks), latent heat storage using phase change materials (PCM), and thermochemical storage.
These are compared in detail in Section~\ref{sec:comp_modeling_methods}, including their representation in MILP models; Table~\ref{tab:tes_comparison} provides a side-by-side summary.
In brief, water tanks dominate deployed installations due to low cost and high technology readiness, while PCM-based systems offer volumetric energy densities 5--14 times higher than water \cite{Sharma2009}, making them attractive when installation space is a binding constraint \cite{Dincer2018}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Battery energy storage systems}
\label{sub:lit_bess_overview}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Battery energy storage systems convert electrical energy to electrochemical potential and back, enabling temporal shifting of both electricity consumption and PV self-consumption.
At commercial building scale, lithium-ion chemistry is the dominant deployed technology.
Key performance parameters include round-trip efficiency (typically 90--96\,\% for Li-ion), the C-rate (ratio of charge or discharge power to nominal energy capacity), and the usable state-of-charge window, which is typically restricted to 10--90\,\% of nominal capacity to preserve cycle life \cite{Gabrielli2018Electrochemical}.
In MILP models, BESS are represented by a linear energy balance tracking the state of charge over time, with constraints on capacity bounds and charge/discharge power limits, as described in Section~\ref{sec:comp_modeling_methods}.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Photovoltaic systems}
\label{sub:lit_pv_overview}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rooftop photovoltaic systems convert in-plane solar irradiance into DC electrical power.
The output at each time step depends on irradiance, cell temperature, and an overall system efficiency that accounts for panel degradation, soiling, wiring losses, and inverter conversion.
The PVWatts model \cite{Dobos2014PVWatts} is a widely validated approach that computes hourly AC output from standard meteorological inputs and is routinely applied in building-level energy studies.
In integrated building energy systems, the temporal variability of PV generation is a primary driver of storage sizing: midday surpluses that cannot be consumed locally are exported to the grid, stored electrically in the BESS, or converted to heat via the HP to charge the TES.

\section{Modeling methods of complex energy systems}
\label{sec:lit_modeling_methods}
% ============================================================

The multi-technology system described in Section~\ref{sec:lit_building_systems} requires a mathematical framework capable of representing the physical behaviour of each component and resolving the interactions between them simultaneously.
\textit{Modeling} such a system means translating physical laws and economic constraints into a formulation that a solver can optimise: variables represent decisions (e.g., how much heat to produce at each hour), equations enforce physical balances (e.g., energy conservation), and an objective function captures the goal (e.g., minimising total cost of ownership).

A central challenge in this process is the treatment of \textit{non-linearities}.
Many physical relationships are inherently non-linear: the COP of an HP varies non-linearly with ambient temperature and partial load ratio, and battery efficiency depends non-linearly on its state of charge.
A \textit{linear} model approximates these relationships as straight lines, sacrificing some physical accuracy in exchange for computational tractability and the guarantee of a globally optimal solution.
A \textit{non-linear} model preserves the exact physics but is far more expensive to solve and typically cannot guarantee a global optimum.
The choice of modeling method therefore reflects a fundamental trade-off between accuracy and solvability.
The remainder of this section first situates available tools within a classification framework, then surveys the principal optimization paradigms, and finally argues why MILP is the most appropriate choice for this study (Section~\ref{sub:milp_advantages}).

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Classification of energy system optimization tools}
\label{sub:lit_tool_classification}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Before surveying individual optimization methods, it is useful to situate them within a broader taxonomy.
Two complementary classification frameworks from the literature structure the landscape of energy system modeling tools.

Ringkj{\o}b et al.~\cite{Ringkjob2018} review 75 modeling tools and classify them along two independent axes: \textit{purpose} (investment decision support, operational dispatch, scenario analysis, power system analysis) and \textit{methodology} (simulation, linear programming, MILP, NLP, heuristic optimization).
Their analysis underscores that the choice of modeling method is driven primarily by the research objective rather than by system complexity alone; complexity acts as a secondary constraint that determines which methods remain computationally tractable.

A complementary classification, oriented toward distributed energy systems at site and building scale, is proposed by Timmerman et al.~\cite{Timmerman2014}, who distinguish five model categories based on their primary analytical focus:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Energy system evolution models} (e.g., TIMES, MARKAL, OSeMOSYS): optimise least-cost investment pathways over multi-decade horizons, typically at national or regional scale.
    Their long time horizon and coarse temporal resolution make them unsuitable for capturing the hourly dispatch dynamics that determine storage value at building scale.

    \item \textbf{Energy system optimization models} (e.g., MILP-based frameworks): co-optimise system configuration and hourly operation over a single representative year.
    This class is the most directly applicable to the simultaneous sizing-and-dispatch problem studied in this thesis.

    \item \textbf{Energy system simulation models} (e.g., EnergyPLAN, HOMER): evaluate the performance of a user-defined configuration through detailed hourly simulation.
    Because the configuration is fixed before simulation, these tools do not guarantee an optimal design; an external search procedure is required to find a good configuration.

    \item \textbf{Energy system accounting models} (e.g., RETScreen): provide rapid pre-feasibility screening of a proposed configuration against a reference case, using simplified operation models.
    Suitable for early-stage assessment but insufficient for investment optimization.

    \item \textbf{Energy system integration models} (e.g., OSMOSE, EINSTEIN): apply Pinch Analysis to maximise heat recovery between process streams.
    Their primary domain is industrial process integration rather than building-scale investment sizing.
\end{itemize}

For the research objective of this thesis --- simultaneously determining the cost-optimal installed capacities and hourly dispatch of an HP, PV array, BESS, and TES for a commercial building --- the \textbf{energy system optimization model} class is the appropriate choice.
Within this class, MILP-based formulations are the dominant framework~\cite{Ringkjob2018, Timmerman2014}, for the reasons discussed in the following subsections.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Linear programming (LP)}
\label{sub:lp}

Linear Programming is the mathematical foundation on which MILP is built.
An LP model minimises (or maximises) a linear objective function subject to a set of linear equality and inequality constraints, with all decision variables being continuous and non-negative.
Because the feasible region is a convex polytope and the objective is linear, LP solvers (e.g., the Simplex method or interior-point methods) are guaranteed to find a \textbf{globally optimal solution} in polynomial time --- a property that directly carries over to the LP relaxation step inside branch-and-bound MILP solvers.

\subsubsection*{Role in energy system modeling}

In energy system optimization, LP is a natural fit for \textbf{operational dispatch problems} where the system topology and component sizes are already fixed.
Given known capacities, the hourly power flows between components can be determined by minimising operating cost subject to energy balance constraints --- all of which are linear.
Tools such as EnergyPLAN and oemof-solph use LP as their core dispatch engine for exactly this reason \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}.

\subsubsection*{Why LP alone is insufficient for sizing}

A common question is whether LP could also handle investment sizing: after all, a continuous capacity variable \texttt{cap\textsubscript{PV}} $\geq 0$ can simply be driven to zero by the solver if PV is not economic.
In practice, however, realistic sizing problems violate LP's all-continuous requirement in at least two ways.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Fixed investment costs.}
    Installing any physical component typically incurs a fixed cost (installation labour, grid connection fees, permitting) that is independent of the installed capacity.
    The resulting cost structure takes the form
    \begin{equation}
        C_{\text{invest},i} = C_{\text{fix},i} \cdot y_i + c_{\text{cap},i} \cdot \mathit{cap}_i,
    \end{equation}
    where $y_i \in \{0,1\}$ is a binary variable that equals 1 if component $i$ is installed and 0 otherwise.
    Without this binary, an LP solver can exploit a spuriously low cost by setting $\mathit{cap}_i$ to an arbitrarily small positive value, paying a negligible fraction of $C_{\text{fix},i}$ while still dispatching the component.
    The binary variable enforces the physical reality that a component is either fully committed (and its fixed cost paid) or absent entirely.

    \item \textbf{Minimum operating thresholds.}
    Heat pumps, gas boilers, and similar equipment cannot operate below a minimum load fraction (typically 20--30\,\% of rated capacity).
    Modeling this requires the constraint $\dot{Q}_t \geq \dot{Q}_{\min} \cdot u(t)$, where $u(t) \in \{0,1\}$ is a binary on/off status variable.
    LP has no binary variables, so it cannot prevent the solver from dispatching a component at arbitrarily small output --- a physically unrealistic result that also distorts the economic dispatch.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent As soon as either condition applies --- and in practice both almost always do --- at least one binary variable is required, and the problem becomes a \textbf{Mixed-Integer Linear Program (MILP)}.
LP therefore occupies a well-defined but narrow role: it is the correct tool for pure operational scheduling of a fully pre-specified system, and it is the inner solver at every node of the MILP branch-and-bound tree.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Non-linear programming}
These methods are used when the system's physical laws are mathematically non-linear and an exact representation is preferred over the linearization required by MILP.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mixed-Integer Non-Linear Programming (MINLP)}: The direct alternative to MILP. It allows for \textbf{non-linear expressions} (like squares or products of variables) while retaining binary variables to model discrete decisions (e.g., component sizing or on/off status). 
    \item \textbf{Non-Linear Programming (NLP)}: Similar to MINLP but excludes integer variables. It is faster than MINLP but cannot be used for sizing problems. Used for \textbf{operational optimization} after component sizing is fixed.
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Programming (DP)}: DP exploits \textit{Bellman's principle of optimality} \cite{bellman1957dynamic}: an optimal sequence of decisions has the property that, whatever the current state, all remaining decisions must themselves be optimal for that state.
    This means past decisions never need to be re-examined, so the problem can be solved \textbf{backwards in time}, one time step at a time.

    \textit{Working principle --- battery dispatch example.}
    Consider dispatching a single battery over $T$ hourly time steps to minimise electricity cost, given known time-varying prices.
    The \textbf{state} at each time step is the battery's State of Charge (SoC), discretised into $N$ levels.
    Define the \textbf{value function} $V(s,\,t)$ as the minimum electricity cost from step $t$ to the end of the horizon, given that the battery is at SoC level $s$ at time $t$.
    The Bellman recursion is:
    \begin{equation}
        V(s,\,t) = \min_{a}\Bigl[\,c(s,\,a,\,t) + V(s',\,t+1)\Bigr],
        \label{eq:bellman}
    \end{equation}
    where $a$ is the chosen charge/discharge action, $s'$ is the resulting next SoC, and $c(s,\,a,\,t)$ is the immediate electricity cost of that action.
    The algorithm fills the $N \times T$ value table starting from $V(s,\,T)=0$ (no future cost at the final step) and works backwards to $t=1$.
    The optimal dispatch schedule is then read forward from the initial SoC.
    Because the table covers every possible state, the solution is \textbf{globally optimal} --- not a near-optimal approximation.

    \textit{Numerical illustration.}
    Consider a battery with three discrete SoC levels --- 0\,kWh, 5\,kWh, and 10\,kWh --- operating over three time steps with electricity prices \mbox{\euro0.30/kWh}, \mbox{\euro0.10/kWh}, and \mbox{\euro0.25/kWh} at $t=1,2,3$ respectively.
    A fixed demand of 5\,kWh must be met each step from the grid or by discharging the battery; the battery may also be charged by buying additional electricity.

    \textbf{Step 1 --- fill $t=3$ (last column).}
    At the final step no future decisions remain, so the value equals the immediate purchase cost only:

    \begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{lll}
    \toprule
    SoC at $t=3$ & Best action & $V(s,\,3)$ \\
    \midrule
    0\,kWh  & Buy 5\,kWh from grid ($5 \times \text{\euro}0.25$) & \euro1.25 \\
    5\,kWh  & Discharge battery, buy nothing               & \euro0    \\
    10\,kWh & Discharge battery, buy nothing               & \euro0    \\
    \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
    \end{table}

    \textbf{Step 2 --- fill $t=2$ using the $t=3$ column.}
    For each SoC, every feasible action is evaluated as $c(s,a,2) + V(s',3)$ and the minimum is stored.
    At SoC\,=\,0 and price \euro0.10/kWh:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Buy only demand (5\,kWh): cost \euro0.50, SoC unchanged $\to$ \euro0.50\,+\,\euro1.25\,=\,\textbf{\euro1.75}
        \item Buy demand + charge battery (10\,kWh): cost \euro1.00, SoC\,$\to$\,5\,kWh $\to$ \euro1.00\,+\,\euro0\,=\,\textbf{\euro1.00} \checkmark
    \end{itemize}
    The algorithm therefore stores $V(0,\,2)=\text{\euro}1.00$ and records ``charge the battery'' as the optimal action.
    The same procedure is applied for SoC\,=\,5 and SoC\,=\,10, completing the $t=2$ column.

    \textbf{Step 3 --- fill $t=1$ and read off the solution.}
    The recursion repeats for $t=1$, this time looking up the already-computed $V(s',\,2)$ values --- no recalculation required.
    Starting from the actual initial SoC the optimal \emph{sequence} of actions is recovered by following the stored decisions forward through the table.
    For this example the solution prescribes: buy only demand at $t=1$ (price high), charge the battery at $t=2$ (price low), discharge at $t=3$ (price medium) --- yielding a total cost of \euro2.50 versus \euro3.00 for the naive strategy of always buying from the grid.

    The completed value table, summarising the minimum cost-to-go $V(s,\,t)$ for every state--time combination, is shown in Table~\ref{tab:dp_example}.
    The optimal path (bold) is traced forward from the initial SoC\,=\,0 at $t=1$ by following the action that achieved each stored minimum.

    \textit{The curse of dimensionality.}
    The power of DP comes at a price: the state must encode \emph{everything} relevant to future decisions.
    For a single battery the state is one-dimensional (SoC), so the table has $N \times T$ entries --- tractable.
    Adding a TES makes the state two-dimensional: (SoC$_\text{BESS}$, SoC$_\text{TES}$) and the table grows to $N^2 \times T$ entries.
    Adding the HP on/off status introduces a third dimension, and so on.
    For the four-component system of this thesis optimised over a full year at hourly resolution, a realistic discretisation yields a state space on the order of:
    \begin{equation*}
        \underbrace{100}_{\text{BESS}} \times \underbrace{100}_{\text{TES}} \times \underbrace{2}_{\text{HP on/off}} \times \underbrace{8{,}760}_{\text{hours}} \approx 175\ \text{million states},
    \end{equation*}
    each requiring the evaluation of all feasible action combinations.
    This \textbf{exponential growth} with the number of state variables --- known as the curse of dimensionality --- renders DP computationally intractable for multi-component systems.
    MILP sidesteps this entirely: rather than enumerating states, it writes the energy balance as a linear constraint for each time step and lets the branch-and-bound solver navigate the full horizon directly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Heuristic and meta-heuristic algorithms}
These methods use iterative search techniques and do \textbf{not guarantee a globally optimal solution}, but can handle highly complex, non-linear, and non-convex problems that MILP and MINLP struggle with.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Genetic Algorithms (GA)}: Inspired by natural selection, it evolves a population of solutions (designs) based on a fitness function (cost/emissions). Often used for simultaneous \textbf{sizing and scheduling} of hybrid renewable systems (PV-BESS).

    \textit{Fitness function:} The fitness function is the mathematical criterion that assigns a scalar score to every candidate design, telling the algorithm which solutions are ``better'' and deserve to reproduce. For energy system sizing it typically aggregates all relevant economic and environmental objectives:
    \begin{equation*}
        f = C_{\text{CAPEX}} + \text{NPV}(C_{\text{OPEX}}) + \text{NPV}(C_{\text{energy}}) - \text{NPV}(R_{\text{feed-in}}) + \sum_k \lambda_k \, v_k
    \end{equation*}
    where $\lambda_k$ is a large penalty weight and $v_k$ is the degree of violation of constraint $k$ (e.g.\ unmet load, battery state-of-charge bounds, or minimum reliability). This \textit{penalty method} steers the search away from infeasible regions without explicitly excluding them from the population. Because evaluating a single candidate requires simulating the full annual dispatch of the system --- typically 8{,}760 hourly time steps --- the fitness evaluation is the computational bottleneck of every GA run. A population of $N$ individuals evaluated over $G$ generations therefore requires $N \times G$ full simulations, which is why GA is generally slower than MILP for the same problem size. The payoff is that it can navigate \textbf{non-convex, non-differentiable} objective landscapes where gradient-based methods fail entirely.

    \textit{Simple analogy:} A GA works like a breeding programme for solutions. It starts with a random population of candidate designs (e.g.\ different combinations of PV capacity, battery size, and dispatch schedule). In each generation, the fittest individuals i.e. those with the lowest cost or emissions, are selected to ``reproduce'': their parameters are combined (\textit{crossover}) and slightly perturbed (\textit{mutation}) to produce offspring. Over many generations, the population converges toward near-optimal solutions, much like how selective breeding improves a trait in animals over successive generations.

    \textit{Example:} Mahesh and Sandhu~\cite{mahesh2017ga} applied a GA to optimally size a grid-connected PV--wind--battery hybrid system. Each individual in the population encodes the number of PV modules, wind turbines, and battery units. The fitness function minimises the total system cost while satisfying a reliability constraint (battery state of charge and power-balance). The GA converges to a Pareto-optimal sizing configuration that outperforms classical rule-based approaches in both cost and reliability.

    \item \textbf{Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)}: Iteratively improves a candidate solution based on the movement of particles in the search space. Used for sizing optimization.

\textit{Simple analogy:} Imagine a flock of birds searching a field for the best patch of food. Each bird (particle) remembers the best location it has personally visited, and also knows the best location found by any bird in the flock. At each time step, every bird adjusts its velocity ,moving partly toward its own best-known spot and partly toward the swarm's global best, gradually converging on the optimal location. In an energy system context, each particle encodes a candidate sizing solution (e.g.\ PV capacity, battery size), and the ``food'' is the minimum cost or maximum reliability.

\textit{Example:} Mohamed et al.~\cite{mohamed2016pso} applied PSO to optimally size a grid-connected hybrid PV--wind--battery--diesel system for remote areas in Saudi Arabia. Each particle represents a combination of component sizes, and the fitness function minimises the \textit{Loss of Load Probability} (LOLP) and total cost simultaneously. A smart-grid load-shifting strategy was integrated to reshape the demand profile, further reducing required storage capacity.
    \item \textbf{Simulated Annealing (SA)}: A probabilistic technique used to approximate the global optimum of a non-convex objective function by iteratively exploring the solution space and occasionally accepting worse solutions to escape local optima.

\textit{Simple analogy:} SA is inspired by the physical process of slowly cooling molten metal. A blacksmith heats steel until the atoms move freely, then cools it gradually so they settle into a low-energy, stable crystalline structure. If cooled too quickly, defects are trapped; if cooled slowly, the atoms find near-perfect arrangements. In optimization, the ``temperature'' controls how willing the algorithm is to accept a worse solution. At high temperature, the algorithm explores broadly and accepts bad moves freely, avoiding entrapment in local optima. As the temperature is gradually reduced (\textit{cooling schedule}), the algorithm becomes increasingly selective, eventually converging on a near-global optimum. This makes SA particularly suited to the \textbf{non-linear, non-convex} sizing problems typical of hybrid renewable energy systems.

\textit{Example:} Ekren and Ekren~\cite{ekren2010sa} applied SA to size a stand-alone PV--wind--battery hybrid system for a campus in Turkey. The decision variables were PV array size, wind turbine rotor swept area, and battery capacity. The objective function minimised total system cost subject to a reliability constraint. 
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Stochastic and hybrid modeling methods}
These methods address the inherent limitations of deterministic MILP related to computational load and parameter certainty.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Stochastic Programming (SP)}: Integrates \textbf{uncertainty} (e.g., volatile electricity prices, uncertain PV output) into the optimization by using multiple scenarios with associated probabilities. Used to determine a system size that is optimal for the \textit{expected cost}.
    \item \textbf{Robust Design Optimization (RDO)}: A specific type of SP that aims to minimize the sensitivity of the solution to external parameters, ensuring the design performs well even under "worst-case" scenarios.
    \item \textbf{Hybrid Optimization Algorithm (HOA)}: Combines the strength of accurate MILP for sizing over \textit{representative days} with a faster heuristic or data-driven model for calculating the \textit{actual annual operating costs}. This offers a major speed advantage, addressing the core computational bottleneck of full-year MILP models.
    \item \textbf{Simulation-Based Optimization}: Couples an accurate black-box simulator (like TRNSYS) with an external meta-heuristic algorithm (GA or PSO) to iteratively search for the optimal design within the simulator’s bounds.
\end{itemize}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Model Predictive Control (MPC)}
\label{sub:mpc}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Model Predictive Control is a widely used operational strategy in building energy management that deserves explicit treatment, because a large body of literature applies it to the same HP--PV--BESS--TES systems studied here.
At each time step, MPC solves a finite-horizon optimization problem --- typically a short-term LP or MILP spanning the next 24--48 hours --- using the current system state as the initial condition and a forecast of prices, weather, and demand as inputs.
Only the first control action is implemented; at the next time step the horizon rolls forward, the state is updated from measurements, and the problem is solved again.
This \textit{receding horizon} structure gives MPC two important practical advantages: it reacts to forecast errors by continuously re-optimizing, and it can enforce physical constraints (SoC bounds, temperature limits) in real time \cite{maier2022mpc}.

\subsubsection*{Fundamental limitation: scheduling without sizing}

Despite its operational strengths, MPC has a critical structural limitation for the purposes of this thesis: \textbf{it takes component sizes as given and optimizes only the scheduling of an already-built system}.
It cannot determine what capacity of HP, BESS, or TES to install, because capacities are fixed parameters rather than decision variables.
This distinction has a practical consequence that is easy to overlook when reading MPC-based studies: a well-tuned MPC controller can demonstrate excellent scheduling performance for a given system, but that performance tells us nothing about whether the system was optimally sized in the first place.
If the TES is too small, no scheduling algorithm --- however sophisticated --- can compensate for the missing storage capacity; if the HP is oversized, the low utilization rate will inflate the annualised capital cost regardless of how cleverly it is dispatched.
In other words, \textbf{claims derived from MPC studies are conditional on the assumed component sizes}: they establish that a certain control strategy works well for a specific configuration, but they cannot be used to conclude that the chosen configuration is economically optimal.

This is precisely the gap that a MILP-based design tool fills.
By co-optimising installed capacities and hourly dispatch in a single problem, MILP ensures that the economic assessment of any technology (e.g., the value of adding TES) reflects its truly optimal size, not an arbitrary or rule-of-thumb specification.
MPC and MILP are therefore complementary rather than competing: MILP is the appropriate tool at the \emph{design stage} to determine what to build; MPC is the appropriate tool at the \emph{operational stage} to control what has already been built.

\subsection{Why MILP is the preferred method for this study}

Several modelling paradigms exist for the analysis and design of energy systems. Ringkj{\o}b et al.
provide an extensive review of 75 modelling tools and classify 
them along two independent axes: \textit{purpose} (investment decision support, 
operation decision support, scenario analysis, power system analysis) and 
\textit{methodology} (simulation, linear programming, mixed-integer linear 
programming, non-linear programming, and heuristic optimisation) \cite{Ringkjob2018}. This 
classification underscores that the choice of modelling method is primarily 
driven by the research objective rather than by system complexity alone; complexity 
acts as a secondary constraint that determines which methods remain computationally 
tractable.


The preceding overview of optimization methods reveals a clear hierarchy of suitability when the objective is to simultaneously size and schedule a multi-technology energy system for a commercial building. This section argues why MILP is the most appropriate framework for the specific system under study, comprising an HP, PV system, BESS, and TES.

\subsubsection*{Global Optimality and Determinism}

The most fundamental advantage of MILP over heuristic and meta-heuristic methods (GA, PSO, SA) is its guarantee of a \textbf{globally optimal solution}. As established before, methods such as GA and PSO do not guarantee global optimality; they converge on near-optimal solutions whose quality depends on population size, tuning parameters, and random initialisation. MILP, by contrast, is a \textbf{deterministic} method: given the same input data, it always returns the same certified optimal solution within a defined optimality gap\footnote{The optimality gap is the relative difference between the best feasible integer solution found (upper bound) and the lower bound on the objective value, expressed as a percentage. The lower bound is obtained via \textit{LP relaxation}: binary variables are temporarily allowed to take fractional values, yielding an easier continuous problem whose objective is always at least as good as any integer-feasible solution. A gap of 0\% certifies global optimality; in practice, solvers such as Gurobi terminate when the gap falls below a user-defined tolerance (e.g.\ 0.01\%).}. This is a critical requirement for an investment decision tool, where the result must be defensible and reproducible~\cite{beck2021milp}.

\subsubsection*{Simultaneous Sizing and Scheduling}

The energy system in this study requires \textbf{coupled sizing and operational optimization}: the optimal capacity of the BESS and TES depends directly on how they are dispatched, and vice versa. MILP naturally handles this coupling in a single mathematical formulation. Binary decision variables represent discrete on/off states of the HP and charging/discharging modes of storage, while continuous variables represent energy flows and component capacities. This avoids the sequential, decoupled approach common in simulation-based methods (e.g., TRNSYS-coupled GA), which may yield suboptimal designs because sizing and scheduling are not optimized simultaneously~\cite{cardoso2014milp}.

This coupling is also what separates MILP fundamentally from Model Predictive Control (MPC), which is widely applied in the building energy literature and is sometimes presented as an alternative to offline optimization.
As discussed in Section~\ref{sub:mpc}, MPC takes component sizes as fixed parameters and optimizes only the operational schedule of an already-built system.
The economic conclusions of MPC studies are therefore conditional on the assumed sizes: a well-controlled but oversized TES will always appear more expensive than a well-controlled, correctly sized one, and an undersized HP will show poor economics regardless of how cleverly it is scheduled.
MILP resolves this ambiguity by determining \emph{both} what to build and how to operate it in a single optimization, ensuring that the economic case for any technology reflects its truly optimal capacity.

\subsubsection*{Native Modeling of Mixed Continuous and Discrete Decisions}

Commercial building energy systems inherently involve both continuous variables (e.g., power flows, state of charge) and discrete decisions (e.g., whether the heat pump is operating, whether the battery is charging or discharging). MILP handles this mixed structure natively, whereas NLP cannot represent discrete decisions, and MINLP while theoretically capable remains computationally intractable for reasonably sized building energy models~\cite{kotzur2018milp}. This makes MILP the standard framework in the literature for building-level multi-energy system optimization~\cite{beck2021milp}.

\subsubsection*{Suitability for TES and HP Modeling}

TES introduces particular modeling challenges: the storage state of charge must be tracked across time steps, charging and discharging cannot occur simultaneously, and heat losses must be accounted for. All of these constraints are naturally expressed as linear equality and inequality constraints in MILP. Cardoso et al.~\cite{cardoso2014milp} demonstrated that MILP can model TES with dual temperature sections, enabling the coupling of a heat pump as a charging technology directly analogous to the HP--TES configuration in this study. Furthermore, Kotzur et al.~\cite{kotzur2018milp} showed that MILP-based optimization frameworks with reduced-complexity TES models solve significantly faster than dedicated simulation tools such as TRNSYS, while retaining sufficient accuracy for investment decisions.

\subsubsection*{Integration of Time-of-Use Electricity Pricing}

A key economic driver for the BESS and TES in a commercial building is \textbf{price arbitrage}: charging storage when electricity is cheap and discharging when it is expensive. MILP is inherently well-suited to this, as time-varying electricity prices enter linearly into the objective function. Beck et al.~\cite{beck2021milp} explicitly note that MILP integrates seamlessly with demand response programs and variable electricity tariffs, making it the most effective framework for cost minimization in building energy systems with flexible loads and storage.

\subsubsection*{Limitations Acknowledged}

A fundamental limitation of MILP is that it requires every physical relationship to be \textbf{linearized} before it can enter the model. A typical and practically important example is the coefficient of performance (COP) of a heat pump, which in reality varies non-linearly with the temperatures of the heat source and sink and with the partial load ratio (PLR). The literature presents three principal strategies for handling this.

The simplest and most widely applied approach is to treat the COP as a \textbf{fixed parameter} a single representative value, typically the rated or season-averaged efficiency. Cardoso et al.~\cite{cardoso2014milp} adopt this assumption in their MILP framework, accepting a modest loss of physical accuracy in exchange for a compact formulation that preserves the global optimality guarantee.

Alternatively, a \textbf{piecewise linear (PWL) approximation} can be used, in which the COP curve is divided into several linear segments, each valid over a subrange of operating conditions. Maier et al.~\cite{maier2022mpc} explicitly compare linear, piecewise linear, and quadratic COP representations within a MILP-based model predictive control framework for buildings, finding that the PWL model improves prediction accuracy over a fixed parameter at the cost of additional binary variables and constraints.

A third approach pre-computes a \textbf{temperature-dependent COP time series} from climate data and feeds it as an exogenous hourly input to the MILP. The COP at each time step is derived from an analytical or regression model as a function of the instantaneous source and sink temperatures, so the resulting expression remains linear in the optimization variables. Ruhnau et al.~\cite{Ruhnau2019} demonstrate this approach by generating hourly heat pump efficiency profiles from ambient temperature time series, while Rogeau et al.~\cite{Rogeau2024HPPL} extend the concept by developing a generic performance mapping methodology that also captures part-load effects.


\section{Mixed-integer linear programming (MILP)}
\label{sub:milp_advantages}

MILP is widely recognized in academic and industrial literature as the state-of-the-art methodology for optimizing the design and scheduling of energy systems\cite{Stinner2021ModelComplexity} \cite{Urbanucci2018Limits}.To understand the advantages of MILP, it is first necessary to understand the underlying computational mechanism used by modern solvers (e.g., Gurobi, CPLEX). 
\subsection{ MILP algorithm}




A \textbf{MILP} model is a mathematical optimization model where the objective function and all constraints are linear, but some, or all, of the decision variables are restricted to be integers (which can include binary, or 0-1, variables). The standard form of an MILP problem is:

\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Minimize (or Maximize)} \quad & \mathbf{c}^T\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{d}^T\mathbf{y} \\
\text{Subject to} \quad & \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{B}\mathbf{y} \le \mathbf{b} \\
& \mathbf{x} \ge 0, \quad \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n \\
& \mathbf{y} \ge 0, \quad \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{Z}^m
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\mathbf{x}$ is a vector of \textbf{continuous} decision variables.
    \item $\mathbf{y}$ is a vector of \textbf{integer} decision variables (often binary variables are used to model yes/no decisions).
    \item $\mathbf{c}$ and $\mathbf{d}$ are vectors of coefficients for the objective function.
    \item $\mathbf{A}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ are matrices of coefficients for the constraints.
    \item $\mathbf{b}$ is a vector of right-hand side values for the constraints.
\end{itemize}

\section*{Key Components}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Linear Objective Function:} The goal (e.g., minimizing cost or maximizing profit) is expressed as a linear combination of the variables. For example, \texttt{Minimize Cost = c1*x1 + c2*x2 + ...}.
    \item \textbf{Linear Constraints:} The limitations and requirements of the problem (e.g., resource availability, demand, capacity) are expressed as linear equations or inequalities. Non-linear expressions like \texttt{x1*x2} are not allowed, though some can be linearized using specific modeling techniques.
    \item \textbf{Mixed Variables:} The defining characteristic is the presence of both continuous variables (which can take any real value) and integer variables (which are restricted to whole numbers).
\end{itemize}


\section*{Solution Approach}

MILP problems are generally NP-hard and more difficult to solve than pure linear programming problems. Solvers (such as CPLEX, Gurobi, or SCIP) typically use algorithms like \textbf{Branch and Bound} or \textbf{Branch and Cut}. These methods work by:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Solving a relaxed version of the problem (ignoring the integer constraints) to get a lower bound on the optimal solution.
    \item Systematically exploring a search tree (branching) and adding constraints (cutting planes) to find an optimal integer-feasible solution.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection{The branching decision (example: PV investment)}

Consider the binary variable for investing in PV system capacity, denoted $y_{\text{PV}} \in \{0, 1\}$.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Initial Problem Node (Root Node):} $\min C_{\text{total}}$ with $0 \le y_{\text{PV}} \le 1$.
    \item \textbf{Branching Operation:} The solver partitions the original problem into two mutually exclusive subproblems:
\end{itemize}

\begin{align*}
    \text{Subproblem 1 (Assigned to Core 1):} \quad & \min C_{\text{total}} \\
    & \text{subject to } y_{\text{PV}} = 1 \quad (\text{PV is Purchased})
\end{align*}

\begin{align*}
    \text{Subproblem 2 (Assigned to Core 2):} \quad & \min C_{\text{total}} \\
    & \text{subject to } y_{\text{PV}} = 0 \quad (\text{PV is Not Purchased})
\end{align*}

\subsection{Parallel processing and pruning}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Parallel Execution:} Core 1 searches for the best design \textit{assuming} PV is bought, while Core 2 searches for the best design \textit{assuming} PV is not bought.
    \item \textbf{Global Update:} If Core 1 quickly finds a feasible solution with total cost $\boldsymbol{C_{\text{Upper}} = \$10,000}$, this value is immediately broadcasted as the new \textbf{Global Upper Bound} on the final cost.
    \item \textbf{Pruning:} If Core 2 finds that the theoretical minimum cost in its branch (its Lower Bound) is $\boldsymbol{L_{\text{Subproblem 2}} = \$10,500}$, Core 2 can immediately \textbf{prune} its entire branch because it cannot contain a solution better than the already known $\boldsymbol{C_{\text{Upper}} = \$10,000}$.
\end{itemize}



\subsection{MILP advantages}
\subsubsection{Guarantee of global optimality}

As a deterministic mathematical programming technique, MILP is fundamentally designed to find the exact best possible outcome for a given objective function.
It guarantees finding a globally optimal solution when the problem converges \cite{Urbanucci2018Limits}, a critical advantage over heuristic methods (such as GA or PSO), which can only guarantee finding acceptable solutions or local optima.

\subsubsection{Integration of discrete and continuous decisions}

MILP is essential for system design and sizing because it effectively integrates the two necessary classes of variables:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Integer/Binary Variables:} These are necessary to model structural and commitment decisions, such as the selection of a technology (e.g., using a HP or not), the existence of a component, the integer number of units to install, or the binary on/off status of a component at each time step.
    \item \textbf{Continuous Variables:} These model the operational schedule and energy flow, such as the electrical power being dispatched or the continuous volume of a storage unit.
\end{itemize}
This dual capability allows MILP to perform the required co-optimization of investment and operation simultaneously \cite{Bordin2020MILP}.

\subsubsection{Handling non-linearity via linearization}

While the presence of complex, high-order non-linear models (e.g., in thermodynamics or equipment performance curves) presents a challenge, MILP addresses this through robust techniques.
The primary method is \textbf{Piecewise Linear (PWL) approximation}.
This technique simplifies non-linear functions (like the thermal dynamics of an Electric Water Heater or the variable efficiency of an inverter) into a series of linear segments, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:pwl_illustration}.
By converting the problem into a strictly linear form, the overall optimization process can be performed by commercially available solvers (e.g., CPLEX, Gurobi) efficiently, providing an "efficiently solvable model" for these complex problems.
MILP's use of this technique is why it remains the preferred approach over complex Mixed-Integer Non-Linear Programming (MINLP) problems, which often have higher computational requirements \cite{Urbanucci2018Limits, Fuso2025University}.

\subsection{Optimization objective functions in MILP}

\subsection*{Cost Minimization (Economic)}

The minimization of total cost is the most common objective, aiming for the economically optimal design and operation.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objective:} Minimize Total Annual Cost ($C_{\text{total}}$) or Lifetime Cost.
    \item \textbf{Key Components:} Total costs comprise Investment Costs ($C_{\text{invest}}$) and Operational Costs ($C_{\text{op}}$).
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item \textbf{Investment Costs ($C_{\text{invest}}$):} The annualized cost is spread over the component's lifetime using an Annuity Factor (ANF). Non-linear cost functions are typically addressed by using discrete equipment sizes or linear approximations.
        $$C_{\text{invest}} = \sum_{i} ANF^{-1} \cdot (C_{\text{inv\_fix},i} + c_{\text{inv\_cap},i} \cdot cap_{i}^{m_{i}})$$
        \noindent where:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $i$ --- index over all system components (HP, PV, BESS, TES, \ldots)
            \item $ANF$ --- Annuity Factor $[-]$, converts a one-time investment into an equivalent annual payment over the component lifetime at a given discount rate
            \item $C_{\text{inv\_fix},i}$ --- fixed investment cost of component $i$ [\euro], independent of its installed capacity (e.g.\ connection costs, installation overhead)
            \item $c_{\text{inv\_cap},i}$ --- specific capacity investment cost of component $i$ [\euro/kW or \euro/kWh], the per-unit cost that scales with size
            \item $cap_{i}$ --- installed capacity of component $i$ [kW or kWh], a continuous decision variable
            \item $m_{i}$ --- cost-scaling exponent of component $i$ $[-]$; $m_i = 1$ gives a linear cost curve, $m_i < 1$ captures economies of scale
        \end{itemize}

        \item \textbf{Operational Costs ($C_{\text{op}}$):} Includes energy costs (based on constant or variable electricity prices, e.g., time-of-use tariffs) and maintenance costs.
        $$ \min C_{\text{total}} = C_{\text{op}} + ANF^{-1} \cdot C_{\text{invest}} $$
        \noindent where:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item $C_{\text{total}}$ --- total annualised system cost [\euro/year], the objective to be minimised
            \item $C_{\text{op}}$ --- annual operational cost [\euro/year], comprising electricity purchase costs, grid tariffs, and maintenance
            \item $ANF^{-1} \cdot C_{\text{invest}}$ --- annualised capital expenditure [\euro/year], spreading the total investment over the component lifetime
        \end{itemize}
    \end{enumerate}
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{CO$_2$ Emissions Minimization (Environmental)}

This objective focuses on reducing the system's environmental impact.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objective:} Minimize Total $\mathbf{CO_2}$ Emissions.
    \item \textbf{Key Components:} Emissions are calculated by weighing the consumption of electricity and other fuels by their respective $\mathbf{CO_2}$ emission factors. This often aims to shift electricity consumption to periods when the grid's carbon intensity is lower. Primary Energy Use (PEU) is sometimes used as a related metric.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Mathematical Formulation}

The overall objective function is the sum of operational and amortized embodied $\text{CO}_2$ emissions:
$$
\min \text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Total}} = \text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Op}} + \text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Emb}}
$$
\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Total}}$ --- total annual $\text{CO}_2$ emissions of the system [kg\,CO$_2$/year], the quantity to be minimised
    \item $\text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Op}}$ --- operational emissions [kg\,CO$_2$/year], arising from electricity drawn from the grid during system operation
    \item $\text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Emb}}$ --- embodied emissions [kg\,CO$_2$/year], representing the manufacturing and construction carbon footprint of each installed component, amortised over its lifetime
\end{itemize}

\textbf{1. Operational Emissions ($\text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Op}}$) - Minimizing Grid Carbon Intensity}

\begin{equation}
\min \text{C}_{\text{CO}_2}^{\text{Op}} = \sum_{t \in T} \left( \sum_{i \in I} \text{E}_{\text{CO}_2,i,t}^{\text{Op}} \cdot \text{P}_{i,t}^{\text{Cons}} \cdot \Delta t \right)
\end{equation}
\noindent where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $t \in T$ --- index over all time steps in the optimization horizon (typically $|T| = 8\,760$ hourly steps per year)
    \item $i \in I$ --- index over all energy-consuming components in the system
    \item $\text{E}_{\text{CO}_2,i,t}^{\text{Op}}$ --- $\text{CO}_2$ emission factor of component $i$ at time step $t$ [kg\,CO$_2$/kWh]; for grid-connected components this equals the hourly marginal carbon intensity of the electricity grid
    \item $\text{P}_{i,t}^{\text{Cons}}$ --- electrical power consumed by component $i$ at time step $t$ [kW], a continuous decision variable
    \item $\Delta t$ --- duration of one time step [h], equal to 1\,h for hourly resolution
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Multi-Criteria Optimization}

To balance conflicting objectives like cost minimization and Co2 emmisions minimization, multi-criteria optimization is applied.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Approach:} The most common method is the \textbf{weighted summation method}, which combines normalized objectives into a single linear objective function using weighting factors ($\alpha$, $\beta$).
    \item \textbf{Weighted Sum Objective:}
    $$\min f = \alpha (\mu C) + \beta D$$
    where $C$ is the cost, $D$ is a discomfort index (or other non-cost objective), and $\alpha$ and $\beta$ ($\alpha + \beta = 1$) are the user's preference factors. $\mu$ is a scaling factor for normalization.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Epsilon constrained method to create Pareto front}

The $\epsilon$-constraint method is highly effective for visualizing the \textbf{trade-off} between two conflicting objectives in a \textbf{MILP} problem, such as minimizing the \textbf{Total Annual Cost} ($f_{\text{Cost}}$) and minimizing the \textbf{Total Life Cycle $\text{CO}_2$ Emissions} ($f_{\text{CO}_2}$).

\subsection*{$\epsilon$-Constraint Method in MILP for Cost vs. $\text{CO}_2$}

In this specific case, the Multi-Objective Problem (MOP) is to simultaneously minimize both $f_{\text{Cost}}$ and $f_{\text{CO}_2}$:
$$
\min \left[ f_{\text{Cost}}(\mathbf{x}), f_{\text{CO}_2}(\mathbf{x}) \right] \\
\text{s.t. } \mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}
$$

The solution vector $\mathbf{x}$ includes both continuous variables (e.g., operational energy flows $\text{P}_{i,t}$) and integer/binary variables (e.g., component sizing $\text{Z}_j$, on/off status $u(t)$).
The feasible region $\mathcal{X}$ is defined by constraints, such as:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Investment Constraints:} Component capacities ($\text{Z}_j$) are non-negative and often discrete (MILP is applicable for incorporating discrete decisions).
    \item \textbf{Energy Balance:} Supply must meet demand in every time step.
    \item \textbf{Physical Limits:} Component models (e.g., Heat Pump COP, TES energy balance) must be satisfied.
\end{enumerate}

\subsubsection*{1. Transformation to Single-Objective Problem (SOP)}

The $\epsilon$-constraint method selects one objective as the primary optimization target.
If we choose to \textbf{minimize Total Annual Cost} ($f_{\text{Cost}}$), the $\mathbf{CO_2}$ objective ($f_{\text{CO}_2}$) is converted into a constraint bounded by a user-defined threshold, $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ (the Greek letter $\epsilon$ gives the method its name).

The resulting $\mathbf{SOP}$ solved by the $\mathbf{MILP}$ solver is:
$$
\min f_{\text{Cost}}(\mathbf{x}) \\
\text{s.t. } f_{\text{CO}_2}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \epsilon_{\text{CO}_2} \\
\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{X}
$$

\subsubsection*{2. Generating the Pareto Front (Trade-off Curve)}

The \textbf{Pareto front} is the set of non-dominated solutions where no objective can be improved without worsening the other is generated by systematically varying $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ and solving the MILP for each step.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Determine $\mathbf{CO_2}$ Range (Boundary Solutions):}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Max $\mathbf{CO_2}$ / Min Cost Point:} Solve the MILP to \textbf{only} minimize $f_{\text{Cost}}$.
        \item The resulting total $\text{CO}_2$ emissions, $f_{\text{CO}_2}^{\max}$, defines the largest $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ needed.
        \item This solution usually involves the cheapest, but most $\text{CO}_2$-intensive technologies (e.g., large Auxiliary Heater, small Heat Pump, minimal storage).
        \item \textbf{Min $\mathbf{CO_2}$ / Max Cost Point:} Solve the MILP to \textbf{only} minimize $f_{\text{CO}_2}$.
        \item The resulting total $\text{CO}_2$ emissions, $f_{\text{CO}_2}^{\min}$, defines the most restrictive $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ limit (which might require the largest or most expensive set of components, e.g., large $\mathbf{PV}$ and \textbf{Battery}).
    \end{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Discretization:} The range $\left[ f_{\text{CO}_2}^{\min}, f_{\text{CO}_2}^{\max} \right]$ is divided into a discrete number of steps $N$.
    \item Each step defines a new, tighter $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ value.
    \item \textbf{Iteration and Output:} For each $\epsilon_{\text{CO}_2}$ step, the SOP is solved.
    \item The optimal annual cost $f_{\text{Cost}}^{*}$ is recorded.
\end{enumerate}

This yields a set of points $\left( f_{\text{CO}_2}, f_{\text{Cost}}^{*} \right)$, which when plotted forms the Pareto front shown in Figure~\ref{fig:example_image}, visually representing the \textbf{cost-optimal system design} for every level of \textbf{$\text{CO}_2$ reduction} desired.
The steepness of the curve indicates the marginal cost of achieving further environmental improvement.

\section{Component modeling in optimization frameworks}
\label{sec:comp_modeling_methods}
\subsection{Component model requirements}
To be implemented within a MILP framework, a component model must fundamentally adhere to strict mathematical constraints regarding linearity to ensure the problem remains solvable by standard optimization algorithms~\cite{Krutzfeldt2021}.
Specifically, both the equality and inequality constraints defining the component's behavior, as well as the objective function, must be linear formulations involving real and integer decision variables~\cite{Wu2019}.
Since the actual thermodynamic dynamics of components such as the temperature-dependent COP of heat pumps or the mixing processes in water heaters are inherently nonlinear and complex, they cannot be directly processed by MILP solvers~\cite{Wu2019, Krutzfeldt2021}.
Consequently, these models must undergo linearization or simplification, such as using piecewise linear approximation methods~\cite{Wu2019}, Taylor polynomial approximations~\cite{Krutzfeldt2021}, or simplifying thermal storage to a homogeneous capacity to avoid endogenous optimization problems~\cite{Steen2015}.
Furthermore, the physical system must be linearized temporally through time discretization, breaking continuous operations into distinct time steps to allow for the simultaneous optimization of design and operation~\cite{Krutzfeldt2021}.

\subsection{Lorentz heat pump model implementation} % Now a subsection
\label{sub:lorentz_model}

In energyPRO \cite{EMD_COP_HeatCapacity}, a simple \emph{Lorentz heat pump model} is implemented for calculating the theoretical COP. This model is based on the Carnot cycle, modified to account for the temperature variation during heat transfer by using mean temperatures.

\subsubsection{Theoretical COP calculation} % Now a subsubsection

The theoretical $\text{COP}$ is calculated using Eq.~\eqref{eq:cop_lorentz}, where temperatures are expressed in Kelvin ($\text{K}$):

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{COP} = \frac{T_{\mathrm{High,mean}}}{T_{\mathrm{High,mean}} - T_{\mathrm{Low,mean}}}
\label{eq:cop_lorentz}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Mean temperature of delivered hot water ($T_{\mathrm{High,mean}}$)}

The mean temperature of the delivered hot water is computed using the logarithmic mean temperature over the condensor:

\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{High,mean}} =
\frac{T_{\mathrm{High,out}} - T_{\mathrm{High,in}}}
{\ln\!\left( \dfrac{T_{\mathrm{High,out}} + 273.15}{T_{\mathrm{High,in}} + 273.15} \right)}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T_{\mathrm{High,out}}$ is the outlet water temperature (\textdegree C),
    \item $T_{\mathrm{High,in}}$ is the inlet water temperature (\textdegree C).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Mean temperature of the heat source ($T_{\mathrm{Low,mean}}$)}

\begin{equation}
T_{\mathrm{Low,mean}} =
\frac{T_{\mathrm{Low,out}} - T_{\mathrm{Low,in}}}
{\ln\!\left( \dfrac{T_{\mathrm{Low,out}} + 273.15}{T_{\mathrm{Low,in}} + 273.15} \right)}
\end{equation}

where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $T_{\mathrm{Low,out}}$ is the initial temperature of the heat source (\textdegree C),
    \item $T_{\mathrm{Low,in}}$ is the cooled-down temperature of the heat source (\textdegree C).
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Actual COP and heat capacity calculation}

The heat pump efficiency factor $\eta_{\mathrm{HP}}$ is computed from the stated COP and the theoretical COP under specification conditions (this efficiency can be derived from manufacturers data as in appendix \ref{appendix:datasheet_hthp}):

\begin{equation}
\eta_{\mathrm{HP}} =
\frac{\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{stated}}}{\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{theoretical,spec}}}
\end{equation}

At each timestep, the actual COP becomes:

\begin{equation}
\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{actual}} =
\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{theoretical,actual}} \cdot \eta_{\mathrm{HP}}
\end{equation}

The resulting heat capacity is:

\begin{equation}
\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{heat}} =
\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{actual}} \cdot \dot{E}_{\mathrm{elec}}
\end{equation}

\paragraph*{Limitation on Heat Capacity}




\subsection{Linearization for MILP:} 
When $T_{\mathrm{High,out}}$ is treated as a decision variable in the MILP optimization, the method described in Section~\ref{sub:lorentz_model} cannot be applied directly; a linearization of the resulting expression is therefore necessary, as done by Krutzefeldt et al. \cite{Germanbuiling}. The resulting non-linear energy relation (Eq.~\eqref{eq:taylor}, $P_{el} = Q_{HP} / COP$) is transformed into a linear constraint by applying a multi-dimensional \textbf{Taylor polynomial approximation} around a fixed operation point (OP). The linear approximation $f(x) \approx f(a) + \nabla f(a) \cdot (x-a)$ for the electric power $P_{el, HP, t}$ (function $f$) with state vector $x = (\dot{Q}_{HP, t}, COP_{ t})$ and fixed operation point $a = (\dot{Q}_{OP}, COP_{OP})$ yields the constraint:
\begin{equation}
\begin{split}
\label{eq:taylor}
P_{el,HP,t} = &\frac{\dot{Q}_{OP}}{COP_{OP}} + \frac{1}{COP_{OP}} \cdot (\dot{Q}_{HP,t} - \dot{Q}_{OP}) \\
&- \frac{\dot{Q}_{OP}}{COP_{OP}^{2}} \cdot (COP_{t} - COP_{OP})
\end{split}
\end{equation}
This process enables the inclusion of realistic, state-dependent COP in the MILP model while maintaining linearity. Note that this approach is only needed when the COP contains a decision variable, as only then the relation $\left(P_{el} = Q_{HP} / COP\right)$ is nonlinear in the decision variables. In this thesis, $T_{\mathrm{High,out}}$ is treated as a given parameter, so the method described in Section~\ref{sub:lorentz_model} can be applied directly.

\subsection{Thermal energy storage (TES) modeling}

\subsubsection{Thermal energy storage (TES) technology selection}

Thermal Energy Storage (TES) systems can be implemented using several distinct technologies, each offering a different trade-off between volumetric energy density, cost, complexity, and operating temperature range. The three most relevant categories for building-scale applications are sensible heat storage (typically water tanks), latent heat storage (Phase Change Materials, PCM), and thermochemical storage.

\textbf{Sensible heat storage} using water tanks is the most mature and widely deployed technology. Water has a high specific heat capacity ($c_p \approx 4.18\,\text{kJ/kg·K}$), is inexpensive, non-toxic, and compatible with a broad range of heating and cooling systems. However, the volumetric energy density is relatively low: storing a meaningful quantity of thermal energy requires large tank volumes, which can be a limiting factor in space-constrained commercial building contexts \cite{Dincer2018}. Water tanks are typically operated between 40\,°C and 95\,°C for heating applications and are well-suited to coupling with high-temperature heat pumps (HPs).

\textbf{Latent heat storage} using Phase Change Materials (PCMs) exploits the energy absorbed or released during a phase transition (typically solid-to-liquid). PCMs can achieve volumetric energy densities 5--14 times higher than water for the same temperature lift, depending on the material selected \cite{Sharma2009}. This significantly reduced footprint makes LTES particularly attractive when available installation space is limited. The primary drawbacks are higher material and encapsulation costs, lower thermal conductivity (requiring enhanced heat exchanger designs), and sensitivity to the operating temperature window of the phase transition, which must be aligned with the supply temperature of the coupled heat source or sink \cite{Cabeza2014}. For HP-coupled systems, PCMs with melting points in the range of 55--80\,°C, such as fatty acids or paraffin waxes, are typically considered.

\textbf{Thermochemical storage} offers the highest theoretical energy density but remains largely at the research and pilot scale, with limited commercial availability and significant engineering complexity. It is therefore not considered in building-scale investment models.

Within MILP-based energy system optimization, TES is typically represented by a linear energy balance model that preserves the tractability of the problem \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}. The detailed modeling of stratification, though more physically precise, significantly increases computation time. Studies show that simplified linear models are often sufficiently accurate for operational optimization, as model overestimations and underestimations of temperature changes tend to compensate for each other \cite{Germanbuiling}.

The selection of a TES model for MILP-based optimization therefore involves a fundamental trade-off between \textbf{computational tractability} and \textbf{physical accuracy} \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}, a trade-off that is particularly relevant for HP-coupled applications where temperature and heat loss dynamics directly affect economic dispatch.



\subsubsection{Rule-of-thumb dimensioning}
In conventional planning, sizing the TES often relies on a \textbf{rule of thumb} to ensure reliability:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Principle:} The storage should be able to provide its \textbf{peak thermal power} for a fixed duration, typically \textbf{6 to 7 hours} \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}. This heuristic is widely applied in preliminary design studies as a starting point before detailed optimization.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Constant (size-independent) losses}
For basic MILP operational models, thermal losses ($\dot{E}_{\text{loss}}$) can be simplified to be either neglected or represented by a \textbf{constant factor ($\delta$)} that is independent ofthe physical size ($Q_{\text{N}}$) \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}.

\begin{equation}
Q_{t} = Q_{t-1}  - \delta + \dot{Q}_{\text{in},t} \eta_{\text{in}} \Delta t - \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{out},t}}{\eta_{\text{out}}} \Delta t,
\label{eq:storage-simplified}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align*}
\delta &= U (2 *\frac{\pi d^{2}}{4}+ \pi d h) \left(\Delta T_{\text{H0}} + \Delta T_{\text{C0}}\right)/2
\end{align*}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Energy Balance:} This approach uses the core energy balance equation, but simplifies the loss terms to depend minimally on dynamic state or size \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}.
    \item \textbf{Rationale:} This maintains strict linearity and computational speed, though it is the least physically accurate, as actual losses depend heavily on the temperature differential with the ambient environment.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Stratified thermal storage (STS) 2-zone model}
\label{sub:sts_model}
The model implements a simplified \textbf{2-zone-model} of a large-scale sensible heat storage with ideal stratification for energy system optimization (see Figure~\ref{fig:stratifiedtes}, \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}). This model assumes a cylindrical storage of diameter $d$ and height $h$ containing two perfectly separated bodies of water: a hot zone at temperature $T_{\text{H}}$ and a cold zone at temperature $T_{\text{C}}$. The zone temperatures are fixed to the feed-in and return temperatures of the heating system respectively. When charging or discharging, the thermocline moves up or down. For investment problems, the diameter is typically fixed and the height is used as the sizing variable, keeping all capacity-related expressions linear \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}.

\noindent The geometric and temperature variables introduced above are defined as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $d$ [m]: inner diameter of the cylindrical tank (fixed design parameter).
    \item $h$ [m]: height of the tank (continuous sizing variable; determines nominal capacity).
    \item $T_{\text{H}}$ [\textdegree C]: temperature of the hot zone, equal to the system supply (feed-in) temperature.
    \item $T_{\text{C}}$ [\textdegree C]: temperature of the cold zone, equal to the system return temperature.
    \item $\Delta T_{\text{HC}} = T_{\text{H}} - T_{\text{C}}$ [K]: usable temperature lift; governs the volumetric energy density of the tank.
    \item $\Delta T_{\text{H0}} = T_{\text{H}} - T_{\text{env}}$ [K]: driving temperature difference between the hot zone and the ambient environment, determining heat loss from the occupied upper portion.
    \item $\Delta T_{\text{C0}} = T_{\text{C}} - T_{\text{env}}$ [K]: driving temperature difference between the cold zone and the ambient environment, determining baseline (empty-tank) heat loss.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\textwidth]{images/TES_picture.png}
    \caption{Schematic of the simplified 2-zone model of a stratified thermal storage with constant hot ($T_{\text{H}}$) and cold ($T_{\text{C}}$) temperature regions \cite{Krien2020oemofsolph}.}
    \label{fig:stratifiedtes}
\end{figure}


\subsubsection{Energy balance}

The equation describing the storage content at time step $t$ is the following:

\begin{equation}
Q_{t} = Q_{t-1} (1 - \beta) - \gamma Q_{\text{N}} - \delta + \dot{Q}_{\text{in},t} \eta_{\text{in}} \Delta t - \frac{\dot{Q}_{\text{out},t}}{\eta_{\text{out}}} \Delta t,
\label{eq:storage-stratified}
\end{equation}
with
\begin{align*}
Q_{N} &= \rho V c \Delta T_{\text{HC}} \\
V &= \frac{\pi d^{2}}{4} h \\
\beta &= U \frac{4}{d \rho c} \Delta t \\
\gamma &= U \frac{4}{d \rho c \Delta T_{\text{HC}}} \Delta T_{\text{C0}} \\
\delta &= U \frac{\pi d^{2}}{4} \left(\Delta T_{\text{H0}} + \Delta T_{\text{C0}}\right) \\
U &= \left( \frac{1}{\alpha_{\text{in}}} + \frac{t_{\text{isolation}}}{\lambda_{\text{isolation}}} + \frac{1}{\alpha_{\text{out}}} \right)^{-1}
\end{align*}

The three terms represent:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\delta$: Constant heat losses through the top and bottom surfaces.
    \item $\gamma \cdot Q_{\text{N}}$: Losses through the total lateral surface assuming the storage to be empty (storage is at $T_{\text{C}}$, and $\Delta T_{\text{C0}}$ is the driving temperature difference), dependent on the height of the storage.
    \item $\beta \cdot Q_{t-1}$: Additional losses through the lateral surface that belong to the hot part of the water body, dependent on the state of charge.
\end{itemize}



The diameter $d$ is given and the height can be adapted to adapt the nominal capacity of the storage. With this assumption,\textbf{ all relations stay linear}.

Because of the space that diffuser plates for charging/discharging take up, it is assumed that the storage can neither be fully charged nor discharged, which is parametrised as a minimal/maximal storage level (indicated by the dotted lines in \ref{fig:stratifiedtes}).



\subsubsection{Capacity and boundary constraints}
The model is constrained by the physical capacity of the storage and boundary conditions required for optimization:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Cycle Constraint:} To model continuous operation, such as in a rolling horizon or repeated operation schedule, the stored energy at the last time step ($t_{\text{last}}$) must equal the stored energy at the initial time step ($t_{\text{zero}}$) of the \textbf{full} simulation.
 \begin{equation}
 Q_{t-1} = Q_{t}
\tag{10}
 \end{equation}

\item \textbf{Capacity Limit:} The instantaneous stored energy is bound by the nominal capacity to ensure physical realism.
\begin{equation}
0 \le Q_{t} \le Q_N
 \tag{11}
 \end{equation}
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.5cm}

The use of this simplified model is advantageous for design and operational optimization due to its robustness and linearity within the MILP framework.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Capacity-based loss model (DER-CAM approach)}
\label{sub:dercam_tes}

An alternative formulation proposed by Steen et al.~\cite{Steen2015} preserves the same two-term loss structure as Eq.~\eqref{eq:storage-stratified} --- a dynamic part proportional to $Q_{t-1}$ and a static baseline part --- but replaces the geometry-derived coefficients $\beta$, $\gamma$, $\delta$ with two empirically calibrated scalars $\beta^*$ and $\gamma^*$:
\begin{equation}
    \text{Loss}_{t}
    = \beta^*\, Q_{t-1}
    + \gamma^*\, Q_N \cdot \frac{\Delta T_{\text{C0}}}{\Delta T_{\text{HC}}},
    \label{eq:dercam_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta T_{\text{C0}} = T_C - T_{\text{env}}$ and $\Delta T_{\text{HC}} = T_H - T_C$ are the same temperature differences as in Eq.~\eqref{eq:storage-stratified}, and $Q_N$ [kWh] is now the direct sizing variable (no diameter or height required).
Since $\Delta T_{\text{C0}}/\Delta T_{\text{HC}}$ is a pre-computed scalar, the expression is linear in $Q_{t-1}$ and $Q_N$.
The coefficients $\beta^*$ and $\gamma^*$ play the same physical roles as $\beta$ and $\gamma$ in the stratified model but are calibrated empirically rather than derived from geometry, so no tank diameter needs to be fixed a priori \cite{Steen2015}.

\paragraph{Volume-dependent coefficients and iterative MILP (Omu et al.).}
A limitation of the original Steen et al.\ formulation is that $\beta^*$ and $\gamma^*$ are treated as constants, independent of the tank size that the optimiser ultimately selects.
Omu et al.~\cite{Omu2016} address this by fitting both coefficients to a power-law function of the tank volume $V$ [m$^3$], calibrated against EnergyPlus dynamic simulations over a range of tank sizes:
\begin{equation}
    \beta^* = \gamma^* = 0.0015 \cdot V^{-0.358}.
    \label{eq:omu_coeff}
\end{equation}
Because $V$ is related to the sizing variable $Q_N$ by $V = Q_N / (\rho\,c_p\,\Delta T_\text{HC})$, substituting Eq.~\eqref{eq:omu_coeff} into Eq.~\eqref{eq:dercam_loss} would make the loss term nonlinear in $Q_N$.
Omu et al.\ preserve MILP tractability through an \textbf{iterative framework}: the coefficients are fixed at the value corresponding to the current estimate of $V$, the MILP is solved to obtain a new optimal $V^*$, the coefficients are updated using Eq.~\eqref{eq:omu_coeff}, and the process repeats until $V^*$ no longer changes between iterations (typically 5--20 iterations, starting from an initial guess such as $V_0 = 20\,\text{m}^3$).
Each individual MILP solve therefore remains fully linear; the nonlinearity is resolved across iterations rather than within a single solve.
This approach reduces the difference between modelled and simulated annual storage losses to within 0--6\,\% across tank volumes of 1--25\,m$^3$ \cite{Omu2016}.

\subsection{Photovoltaic (PV) system modeling}
\label{sub:pv_modeling}

Because PV generation enters the MILP as a fixed exogenous time series --- the optimizer dispatches against it but does not change it --- the choice of PV model is a pre-processing decision that trades off physical accuracy against input data requirements and computational effort.
Three levels of modeling complexity are established in the literature, ranging from a single efficiency parameter to a full semiconductor device model.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 1: Simple efficiency model}

The simplest representation expresses instantaneous DC power as a linear scaling of in-plane irradiance by a single system efficiency $\eta_{\text{sys}}$:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{DC},t} = G_{\text{POA},t} \cdot A_{\text{PV}} \cdot \eta_{\text{sys}},
    \label{eq:pv_simple}
\end{equation}
where $G_{\text{POA},t}$ [W/m$^2$] is the irradiance in the plane of the array at time step $t$, $A_{\text{PV}}$ [m$^2$] is the total module area, and $\eta_{\text{sys}}$ [-] is a fixed overall system efficiency lumping panel efficiency, wiring losses, soiling, and inverter conversion into a single scalar.
This model requires only irradiance data and one calibration parameter.
Its main limitation is that it ignores the temperature dependence of panel efficiency: at high irradiance, cell temperature rises and output drops below the isothermal prediction, so the simple model systematically overestimates summer peak generation.
For preliminary sizing studies where exact hourly profiles are less important than seasonal energy totals, this accuracy loss is often acceptable \cite{Ringkjob2018}.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 2: Temperature-corrected model (PVWatts)}

The PVWatts model \cite{Dobos2014PVWatts} extends Eq.~\eqref{eq:pv_simple} by correcting for cell temperature $T_{\text{cell}}$ [\textdegree C]:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{DC},t} = \frac{G_{\text{POA},t}}{1{,}000} \cdot P_{\text{STC}} \left(1 + \frac{\gamma_{\text{pdc}}}{100}\left(T_{\text{cell},t} - T_{\text{ref}}\right)\right),
    \label{eq:pvwatts}
\end{equation}
where $P_{\text{STC}}$ [W] is the nameplate DC power at standard test conditions ($G = 1{,}000$\,W/m$^2$, $T_{\text{cell}} = 25$\,\textdegree C), $\gamma_{\text{pdc}}$ [\%/\textdegree C] is the temperature coefficient of power (typically $-0.4$ to $-0.5$\,\%/\textdegree C for silicon modules), and $T_{\text{ref}} = 25$\,\textdegree C.
Cell temperature $T_{\text{cell}}$ is not directly measurable from standard weather stations, so it must itself be modelled from ambient conditions.
Three approaches of increasing complexity are used in practice:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{NOCT model.}
    The Nominal Operating Cell Temperature (NOCT) is a single datasheet parameter defined at standard conditions ($G = 800$\,W/m$^2$, $T_{\text{amb}} = 20$\,\textdegree C, wind speed 1\,m/s).
    Cell temperature is then estimated as:
    \begin{equation}
        T_{\text{cell}} = T_{\text{amb}} + \frac{\text{NOCT} - 20}{800} \cdot G_{\text{POA}}.
        \label{eq:noct}
    \end{equation}
    This is the simplest approach, requiring only one additional datasheet value, but it ignores wind speed and therefore overestimates cell temperature --- and thus underestimates output --- in windy conditions.

    \item \textbf{Sandia back-surface temperature model (PVWatts default).}
    King et al.~\cite{Dobos2012ImprovedCEC} parameterise module back-surface temperature $T_{\text{module}}$ as a function of irradiance and wind speed using two empirical coefficients $a$ and $b$ that depend on the mounting configuration (open rack, close roof mount, etc.):
    \begin{equation}
        T_{\text{module}} = G_{\text{POA}}\,e^{a + b\,v_{\text{wind}}} + T_{\text{amb}},
        \label{eq:sandia_tmod}
    \end{equation}
    and cell temperature is then:
    \begin{equation}
        T_{\text{cell}} = T_{\text{module}} + \frac{G_{\text{POA}}}{1{,}000}\,\Delta T,
        \label{eq:sandia_tcell}
    \end{equation}
    where $\Delta T$ is a small temperature difference between cell and back-surface (typically 1--3\,\textdegree C).
    This model captures the wind-cooling effect and is the default in PVWatts and pvlib.
    Its coefficients are tabulated for common module-mounting combinations by the California Energy Commission \cite{Dobos2012ImprovedCEC}.

    \item \textbf{Faiman heat-balance model.}
    Faiman~\cite{Faiman2008} derives cell temperature from a simplified steady-state energy balance, expressing the net heat loss from the module as a linear function of wind speed:
    \begin{equation}
        T_{\text{cell}} = T_{\text{amb}} + \frac{G_{\text{POA}}}{U_0 + U_1\,v_{\text{wind}}},
        \label{eq:faiman}
    \end{equation}
    where $U_0$ [W/(m$^2$\,K)] is the heat loss coefficient at zero wind (dominated by natural convection and radiation) and $U_1$ [W\,s/(m$^3$\,K)] represents wind-driven forced convection.
    Typical values are $U_0 \approx 25$--$30$\,W/(m$^2$\,K) and $U_1 \approx 6$--$7$\,W\,s/(m$^3$\,K) \cite{Faiman2008}.
    This model has a clear physical interpretation and requires only two fit parameters regardless of mounting type, making it easy to calibrate against measured cell temperature data when available.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent All three sub-models feed $T_{\text{cell}}$ into Eq.~\eqref{eq:pvwatts} and are interchangeable within the PVWatts framework.
The NOCT model is the most conservative in data requirements; the Sandia model is the industry standard for rooftop systems; the Faiman model is preferred when site-measured temperature data are available for calibration.
PVWatts is widely validated for building-scale studies and is the basis for tools such as SAM (System Advisor Model) and pvlib \cite{Dobos2014PVWatts}.
Compared to the simple efficiency model, PVWatts substantially improves annual yield accuracy, particularly during hot summer periods where cell temperature can reduce output by 10--15\,\% relative to the STC rating.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 3: Single-diode model (five-parameter model)}

The single-diode model \cite{DeSoto2006} represents a PV cell as an equivalent electrical circuit comprising a current source, one diode, a series resistance, and a shunt resistance.
It is parameterised by five values extracted from datasheet measurements at standard test conditions (STC: $G_\text{ref} = 1{,}000$\,W/m$^2$, $T_\text{ref} = 25$\,\textdegree C): photocurrent $I_\text{L}$, diode saturation current $I_0$, series resistance $R_s$, shunt resistance $R_\text{sh}$, and diode ideality factor $n$.

\textit{How $G_{\text{POA}}$ is converted to power.}
The conversion proceeds in three steps, repeated at each time step.

\textbf{(1) Translate parameters to actual conditions.}
The five STC parameters are scaled to the instantaneous $G_{\text{POA}}$ and $T_{\text{cell}}$ using semi-empirical relations.
The most important scalings are: photocurrent scales nearly linearly with irradiance (more photons produce more electron--hole pairs), saturation current grows strongly with temperature (thermal excitation), and shunt resistance decreases with irradiance:
\begin{align}
    I_L(G,T)        &= I_{L,\text{ref}}\cdot\frac{G}{G_\text{ref}}\cdot\bigl(1+\alpha_{I_{sc}}(T-T_\text{ref})\bigr), \label{eq:sdm_il}\\
    I_0(T)          &= I_{0,\text{ref}}\cdot\!\left(\frac{T}{T_\text{ref}}\right)^{\!3}\exp\!\left(\frac{E_g}{nk}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_\text{ref}}-\frac{1}{T}\right)\right), \label{eq:sdm_i0}\\
    R_\text{sh}(G)  &= R_\text{sh,ref}\cdot\frac{G_\text{ref}}{G}. \label{eq:sdm_rsh}
\end{align}

\textbf{(2) Solve the implicit $I$--$V$ equation.}
With the translated parameters the full current--voltage characteristic is:
\begin{equation}
    I = I_L - I_0\!\left(\exp\frac{V+IR_s}{nV_T}-1\right) - \frac{V+IR_s}{R_\text{sh}},
    \label{eq:sdm_iv}
\end{equation}
where $V_T = kT/q \approx 0.026$\,V at 25\,\textdegree C is the thermal voltage.
Because $I$ appears on both sides, Eq.~\eqref{eq:sdm_iv} is transcendental and is solved numerically (e.g.\ Newton--Raphson) for a sweep of voltages from 0 to the open-circuit voltage $V_{oc}$.

\textbf{(3) Extract the Maximum Power Point (MPP).}
At each voltage the instantaneous power is $P(V)=V\cdot I(V)$.
An MPP algorithm (Golden Section search or derivative-based) finds the voltage $V_{mp}$ that maximises $P(V)$, giving:
\begin{equation}
    P_{\text{DC}} = V_{mp}\cdot I_{mp}.
    \label{eq:sdm_mpp}
\end{equation}
The full chain is therefore:
\begin{equation*}
G_{\text{POA}},\;T_{\text{cell}}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{scale}}\;
I_L,\,I_0,\,R_\text{sh}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{solve}}\;
I\text{--}V\text{ curve}
\;\xrightarrow{\text{MPP}}\;
P_{\text{DC}}.
\end{equation*}
This procedure is computationally cheap per time step but is called iteratively by an optimizer millions of times during a MILP solve, making it infeasible to embed directly.
In practice the model is always run as a pre-simulation to produce the generation time series fed to the MILP.
Compared to PVWatts, the single-diode model more accurately captures low-irradiance and high-temperature behaviour and can represent partial shading effects through multi-module string configurations.
It is the standard in detailed performance auditing and bankability studies, but the additional accuracy rarely justifies the added complexity for investment-sizing applications where annual energy yield matters more than hourly precision \cite{DeSoto2006}.

\subsection{Battery energy storage systems (BESS)}
\label{sub:battery_modeling}

A BESS converts electrical energy to electrochemical potential and back, enabling temporal shifting of PV surplus and grid-price arbitrage.
Four levels of modeling complexity appear in the literature, ranging from a simple linear energy balance to detailed electrochemical models.
The choice governs both the physical accuracy of the dispatch prediction and the number of additional variables and constraints added to the MILP.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 1: Linear energy balance (standard MILP approach)}
\label{subsub:bess_linear}

The dominant approach in building-scale MILP studies \cite{Gabrielli2018Electrochemical, cardoso2014milp} tracks the State of Charge (SoC) through a linear energy balance with constant round-trip efficiencies:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SoC}_t = \text{SoC}_{t-1} + P^{ch}_t\,\eta^{ch}\,\Delta t - \frac{P^{dis}_t}{\eta^{dis}}\,\Delta t \quad \forall\, t \in T,
    \label{eq:bess_linear}
\end{equation}
where $P^{ch}_t$ and $P^{dis}_t$ [kW] are the charge and discharge power flows, $\eta^{ch}$ and $\eta^{dis}$ [-] are the one-way efficiencies (typically 0.95--0.98 for Li-ion), and $\Delta t$ [h] is the time step duration.
Physical limits are enforced through:
\begin{align}
    \text{SoC}_{\min} &\leq \text{SoC}_t \leq \text{SoC}_{\max}, \label{eq:bess_soc_lim}\\
    P^{ch}_t         &\leq P^{\text{nom}},\quad P^{dis}_t \leq P^{\text{nom}}. \label{eq:bess_pow_lim}
\end{align}
A key practical observation is that a binary mutual-exclusion variable ($u_t \in \{0,1\}$ preventing simultaneous charge and discharge) is not needed when $\eta^{ch}\cdot\eta^{dis} < 1$: the optimizer will never charge and discharge simultaneously because the round-trip loss makes it strictly suboptimal, and the LP relaxation already prevents it within the chosen MIP gap \cite{Gabrielli2018Electrochemical}.
This saves one binary variable per time step (8,760 for a full-year model), meaningfully reducing solve time.

Self-discharge can be incorporated by multiplying $\text{SoC}_{t-1}$ by a retention factor $(1-\eta^{SD})$, where $\eta^{SD}$ is the fractional energy lost per time step:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SoC}_t = \text{SoC}_{t-1}(1-\eta^{SD}) + P^{ch}_t\,\eta^{ch}\,\Delta t - \frac{P^{dis}_t}{\eta^{dis}}\,\Delta t.
    \label{eq:bess_selfdischarge}
\end{equation}
For Li-ion batteries self-discharge is typically below 0.1\,\%/day and is often neglected in hourly models; omitting it slightly overestimates annual storage performance.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 2: C-rate dependent efficiency}
\label{subsub:bess_crate}

The constant-efficiency assumption in Model~1 holds well when the battery charges and discharges slowly relative to its capacity.
At high C-rates --- defined as $C_t = P_t / E_{\text{nom}}$ [h$^{-1}$], i.e.\ the ratio of instantaneous power to installed energy capacity --- internal resistive losses increase and the effective round-trip efficiency drops noticeably \cite{Bobanac2021}.

A critical modeling subtlety arises in \textbf{sizing} problems.
PWL approximation can represent a nonlinear efficiency curve $\eta(C)$ when $C$ is a function of a single decision variable --- which is the case in a pure \textbf{operational} problem where $E_{\text{nom}}$ is a fixed parameter and $C_t = P_t / E_{\text{nom}}$ is therefore linear in the only unknown $P_t$.
In a \textbf{joint sizing-and-dispatch} MILP, however, both $P_t$ and $E_{\text{nom}}$ are decision variables, making $C_t = P_t / E_{\text{nom}}$ a ratio of two unknowns --- a bilinear expression that cannot be handled by standard PWL linearization.
Embedding C-rate-dependent efficiency in a sizing MILP would therefore require an iterative outer loop analogous to the approach of Omu et al.\ for TES losses (Section~\ref{sub:dercam_tes}): fix $E_{\text{nom}}$, solve the MILP, update the efficiency curve, and repeat until convergence.
This additional complexity is rarely justified for building-scale daily-cycling applications, where the efficiency variation across the feasible C-rate range is modest \cite{Bobanac2021}, and Model~1 with a single representative round-trip efficiency is the standard in the literature \cite{Gabrielli2018Electrochemical, cardoso2014milp}.

% ----
% ----
\subsubsection{Model 3: Degradation modeling}
\label{subsub:bess_aging}

Battery capacity fades over time through two mechanisms: \textbf{calendar aging} (time- and temperature-dependent degradation regardless of usage) and \textbf{cycle aging} (capacity loss proportional to cumulative charge throughput and cycle depth).
Neglecting degradation in a long-horizon investment model leads to underestimation of the required installed capacity, since the optimizer assumes the battery retains its initial energy capacity throughout its lifetime.

The simplest linear degradation proxy adds a throughput cost to the objective function:
\begin{equation}
    C_{\text{deg}} = c_{\text{deg}} \sum_{t} \left(P^{ch}_t + P^{dis}_t\right)\Delta t,
    \label{eq:bess_deg}
\end{equation}
where $c_{\text{deg}}$ [\euro/kWh] is the cost per unit of energy throughput, calibrated as $c_{\text{deg}} = C_{\text{Bat}} / \Delta E_{\text{Bat}}$ with $C_{\text{Bat}}$ the battery replacement cost and $\Delta E_{\text{Bat}}$ the expected lifetime energy throughput \cite{Xu2018BatteryAging}.
This penalises excessive cycling without adding any binary variables.
It corresponds to the one-segment special case of the piecewise linear cycle-aging cost model proposed by Xu et al.~\cite{Xu2018BatteryAging}, which more generally segments the cycle-depth range into $J$ intervals each with a separate marginal aging cost, allowing the model to capture the nonlinear dependence of capacity fade on depth of discharge.
Increasing the number of segments improves accuracy at the cost of additional binary variables and state-tracking constraints; the $J=1$ throughput cost is therefore the simplest tractable option when computational efficiency is the priority.

\subsection{Inverter modeling}
\label{sub:inverter_modeling}
Modeling the \textbf{inverter efficiency} in a \textbf{MILP model} is crucial for accurately calculating energy losses and finding the true economic optimum, especially for systems including Photovoltaic ($\text{PV}$) or Battery Energy Storage Systems ($\text{BESS}$). Since inverter efficiency is non-linear—it varies significantly with the input power load—you must use \textbf{linearization techniques} to keep the problem solvable with $\text{MILP}$.The most common and effective method is \textbf{Piecewise Linear ($\text{PWL}$) Approximation}.
\subsection*{PWL Approximation for Inverters}
The core idea is to approximate the smooth, non-linear efficiency curve of the inverter with a series of straight-line segments. This converts the overall non-linear problem into a solvable $\text{MILP}$ formulation by introducing auxiliary variables and constraints.
\subsubsection*{1. The Need for Linearization}
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{The Problem:} Inverter output power ($P_{ac}$) is the result of input DC power ($P_{dc}$) multiplied by efficiency ($\eta$): $P_{ac} = P_{dc} \cdot \eta(P_{dc})$. 
Since $\eta$ itself is a function of $P_{dc}$. Consider for means of example the PVwatt model \cite{Dobos2014PVWatts}.

\begin{equation}
\eta = \frac{\eta_{\text{nom}}}{\eta_{\text{ref}}} - 0.0162 \cdot \zeta - \frac{0.0059}{\zeta} + 0.9858 \quad \text{where} \quad \zeta = \frac{P_{\text{dc}}}{P_{\text{dc}0}} \quad \text{and} \quad P_{\text{dc}0} = \frac{P_{\text{ac}0}}{\eta_{\text{nom}}} 
\end{equation}

 this equation is \textbf{non-linear} and incompatible with linear programming ($\text{LP}$) and standard $\text{MILP}$ solvers.
 \begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{images/inverter effiency.png}
    \caption{Inverter Part-Load Efficiency. This graph illustrates how inverter efficiency varies with the load fraction, showing different nominal efficiencies and a comparison to PVWatts V1.}
    \label{fig:inverter_efficiency}
\end{figure}
 
 \item \textbf{The Trade-off:} Assuming a constant efficiency (a simple linear model) is fast but leads to modeling inaccuracies and suboptimal economic results. $\text{PWL}$ modeling offers a better balance between \textbf{accuracy} and \textbf{computational complexity}.\end{itemize}\subsubsection*{2. Implementation Steps}The \textbf{Piecewise Linear Approximation ($\text{PWL}$)} transforms the curve into a set of linear functions, typically using the \textbf{Multiple Choice/Convex Combination approach} (also known as the $\lambda$-method or Special Ordered Sets of Type 2, $\text{SOS}2$):\begin{enumerate}\item \textbf{Define Segments/Breakpoints:} The non-linear curve is divided into $N$ linear segments defined by $N+1$ known points (breakpoints) along the input power axis. The accuracy increases with the number of segments, but so does the computational time.\item \textbf{Introduce Binary Variables (for segments):} You assign auxiliary $\text{binary variables}$ ($\delta_s$) to identify which segment is active at any given time step.\item \textbf{Use Convex Combination ($\lambda$-method):} The actual power flow point on the curve is represented as a convex combination of the segment breakpoints. This uses additional continuous variables ($\lambda_i$) whose sum must equal 1.
 \begin{itemize}
 \item $P_{dc} = \sum_{i} \lambda_i P_{dc, i}$ (The actual power is a weighted average of the breakpoint powers).
 \item $\eta(P_{dc}) = \sum_{i} \lambda_i \eta_i$ (The calculated efficiency is a weighted average of the breakpoint efficiencies).
 \end{itemize}
 Constraints ensure that only two adjacent $\lambda$ values (or their corresponding segment binary variables) can be non-zero simultaneously, forcing the solution to lie on a defined segment.
 \end{enumerate}

\subsection{Heat pump part-load modeling}
\label{sub:part_load_modeling}

The temperature-dependent COP models in Sections~\ref{sub:lorentz_model} and \ref{sub:milp_advantages} describe full-load performance --- what the HP delivers when operating at its rated capacity under a given set of source and sink temperatures.
In practice, building heat demand rarely matches the HP's full-load output, so the machine operates at a \textbf{part-load ratio} (PLR $< 1$) for a significant fraction of annual hours.
Part-load operation reduces efficiency below the full-load COP, and the magnitude of this reduction depends strongly on whether the HP uses a fixed-speed compressor (on/off cycling) or a variable-speed inverter drive.
Three modeling approaches of increasing physical detail are used in the literature.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 1: No part-load correction (full-load COP assumed)}

The simplest approach uses a single representative COP at all operating points, ignoring any efficiency degradation at partial load.
This is the implicit assumption when a fixed or temperature-dependent COP time series is applied without a PLR correction.
It overestimates annual performance whenever the HP operates below full load, which is the majority of heating hours in a mild climate.
The error is larger for fixed-speed machines (where cycling losses dominate) and smaller for inverter-driven units (where modulation is efficient down to approximately 30--40\,\% of rated capacity).
Despite this limitation, the no-correction assumption is frequently used in investment-sizing MILP studies where the COP is already represented as an hourly pre-computed array and adding a PLR correction would introduce a bilinear term \cite{cardoso2014milp}.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 2: On/off cycling model (EN 14511 degradation coefficient)}

For fixed-speed compressors, or as a conservative approximation for inverter units, the EN~14511:2022 standard defines a part-load COP correction using a degradation coefficient $C_d$ \cite{Grassi2018HP}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pl}
\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{PL}} = \left(1 - C_d\,(1 - \mathrm{PLR})\right)\,\mathrm{COP}^{\mathrm{FL}}
\quad\text{with}\quad
\mathrm{PLR} = \frac{\min(\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{demand}},\,\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{duty}})}{\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{duty}}},
\end{equation}

where $C_d = 0.25$ for air-to-water systems \cite{Grassi2018HP} and $\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{duty}}$ [W] is the maximum heat output achievable under the prevailing ambient conditions:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:duty}
\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{duty}} = \mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{Lorentz}}\,\frac{\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{HP,nom}}}{\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{nom}}}.
\end{equation}

The electrical power drawn and net heat delivered are then:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:duty_final}
P_{\mathrm{HP}} = \frac{\min(\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{demand}},\,\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{duty}})}{\mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{PL}}}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\dot{Q}_{\mathrm{HP}} = \mathrm{COP}_{\mathrm{PL}}\,P_{\mathrm{HP}}.
\end{equation}

A critical subtlety arises in \textbf{sizing} problems.
$\dot{Q}_\text{duty}$ depends on the installed capacity $\dot{Q}_\text{HP,nom}$, which is a decision variable:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{PLR} = \frac{\dot{Q}_\text{demand}}{\dot{Q}_\text{duty}} = \frac{\dot{Q}_\text{demand}\,\mathrm{COP}_\text{nom}}{\mathrm{COP}_\text{Lorentz}\,\dot{Q}_\text{HP,nom}}.
\end{equation}
This is a ratio of a parameter to a decision variable --- a bilinear expression that cannot be embedded directly as a linear MILP constraint.
The EN~14511 part-load correction is therefore only strictly linear when capacity is fixed (a pure operational problem).
In a joint sizing-and-dispatch MILP, an alternative formulation is required to avoid this nonlinearity; the specific approach adopted in this thesis is described in Section~\ref{sec:component_models}.

% ----
\subsubsection{Model 3: Inverter-modulated performance map (Rogeau)}

Modern inverter-driven heat pumps operate in three distinct regimes depending on ambient temperature \cite{Rogeau2024HPPL}:

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cop_star_delta_t_new}
\mathrm{COP}^{\star}(\Delta T) =
\begin{cases}
\mathrm{COP}^{\mathrm{FL}}(\Delta T_{\mathrm{HP}}), & T_{\mathrm{out}} \le T_{\mathrm{biv}} \\
\mathrm{COP}^{\mathrm{PL}}(\Delta T_{\mathrm{HP}}), & T_{\mathrm{biv}} < T_{\mathrm{out}} \le T_{\mathrm{biv},2} \\
\mathrm{COP}^{0/1}(\Delta T), & T_{\mathrm{out}} > T_{\mathrm{biv},2}
\end{cases}
\end{equation}

Below the first bivalence temperature $T_{\mathrm{biv}}$ the compressor runs at full speed; between the two bivalence temperatures the inverter modulates power continuously, achieving a higher average COP than on/off cycling; above $T_{\mathrm{biv},2}$ the heat demand is so low that on/off cycling resumes.
Rogeau et al.~\cite{Rogeau2024HPPL} develop a generic performance mapping methodology that captures all three regimes from manufacturer test data and pre-computes a complete hourly COP and capacity-fraction time series for use in optimization.

Embedding this three-regime model directly in a MILP requires binary variables to select the active regime at every time step, plus PWL approximations for the performance curves in each regime.
As Maier et al.~\cite{maier2022mpc} show, this significantly increases problem size and solve time.
For investment-sizing applications the common simplification is therefore to adopt Model~2 or to collapse all regimes to a single on/off binary with the full-load COP, acknowledging that this underestimates the seasonal COP of inverter-driven units.

\section{Conclusion}
The literature reviewed in this chapter surveys the candidate modeling strategies for integrated building energy systems and establishes the methodological foundation for the design tool developed in this thesis.
The review identifies MILP-based co-optimization as the most appropriate framework for the joint sizing-and-dispatch problem, for the reasons summarised in Table~\ref{tab:method_comparison}: it is the only approach that simultaneously guarantees a globally optimal solution, handles discrete investment decisions, and integrates naturally with time-varying electricity tariffs.
The component-level models reviewed --- stratified TES representations, Lorentz-based and part-load HP models, and PWL linearizations for inverter efficiency --- demonstrate that sufficient physical realism can be retained within a strictly linear formulation.

\subsection*{Gaps identified in the existing literature}

Despite this methodological clarity, the literature review also reveals important gaps that motivate the present work.

\textbf{Scheduling studies cannot substitute for design studies.}
A large fraction of the published work on HP--PV--BESS--TES systems uses Model Predictive Control or simulation-based approaches to evaluate operational strategies for a system whose component sizes are fixed in advance, typically by a rule of thumb or a normative standard such as EN~15450.
As discussed in Section~\ref{sub:mpc}, the economic conclusions of these studies are conditional on the assumed sizes: a well-controlled but wrongly sized system will always appear either too expensive or insufficiently reliable, regardless of how sophisticated the scheduling algorithm is.
From the existing MPC and simulation literature it is therefore \textbf{not possible to determine the truly optimal combination of technologies} for a given building application, because sizing and scheduling have not been resolved simultaneously.

\textbf{Commercial buildings are underrepresented.}
The majority of MILP-based design studies focus on residential single-family dwellings or on large industrial and district-level systems.
The commercial building segment --- characterised by daytime-dominated electrical loads, process heat demands at elevated temperatures, and large roof areas relative to floor area --- occupies a distinct operating point that is not directly transferable from either end of this spectrum.
Optimal technology combinations and storage sizing ratios identified for residential systems may therefore be structurally different for commercial applications.

\textbf{Scenario robustness is rarely assessed.}
Studies that do perform optimal sizing typically fix a single set of techno-economic parameters (electricity price, grid carbon intensity, equipment costs) and report a single optimal design.
Yet the commercial viability of a capital-intensive investment in HP, PV, BESS, and TES depends critically on assumptions about future energy prices and carbon policy that are inherently uncertain.
Without a systematic scenario analysis, it is impossible to assess whether a recommended design remains economically sound under plausible alternative futures or is highly sensitive to a single parameter.

\subsection*{Research question}

These gaps lead directly to the research question addressed in this thesis:

\begin{quote}
\textit{What is the cost-optimal combination and sizing of a high-temperature heat pump, PV system, battery storage, and thermal energy storage for a typical Belgian commercial building, and how robust is this design across plausible future energy and policy scenarios?}
\end{quote}

Answering this question requires a MILP-based design tool that co-optimises investment and dispatch simultaneously, applied to a representative commercial building case study (M-accent), and evaluated across a structured set of scenarios covering electricity price trajectories, grid carbon intensity, and equipment cost developments.
The following chapters develop this tool and present the results.

The preceding chapters established the problem context and the theoretical foundation for this work.
Chapter~\ref{intro} identified the core challenge: replacing the two 80\,kW gas boilers at the M-accent commercial building with an electrified hybrid system comprising an HP, PV array, BESS, and TES, while simultaneously minimising the TCO.
Chapter~\ref{literature review} reviewed the available optimisation paradigms and concluded that MILP is the most appropriate framework for this application: it guarantees a globally optimal solution, natively handles the mixed continuous-and-discrete decision structure inherent to energy systems, and integrates seamlessly with the time-varying electricity tariffs that drive economic dispatch decisions.

Building on those foundations, this chapter presents the complete mathematical formulation of the MILP design tool developed in this thesis.
The model co-optimises two interrelated decision layers: the \textit{investment layer}, which determines the optimal installed capacities of the HP, PV array, BESS, and TES; and the \textit{operational layer}, which determines the cost-optimal hourly dispatch of every technology over a representative full calendar year (8\,760 time steps).
These two layers cannot be solved independently: the economic value of storage, for instance, depends on how it is operated, and the optimal operating strategy in turn depends on the installed capacity.
The MILP formulation resolves this coupling in a single unified optimisation problem.

\section{System overview and design tool architecture}
\label{sec:system_overview}
% ============================================================

The design tool follows a four-stage workflow, illustrated in
Figure~\ref{fig:milp_methodology}.

% ── FIGURE ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/MILP_Methodology_Diagram.png}
    \caption{
        Overview of the MILP-based design optimisation framework developed in this thesis.
        The four stages proceed from data assembly and pre-processing, through MILP
        formulation and solving, to the reporting of optimal capacities.
    }
    \label{fig:milp_methodology}
\end{figure}

In the first stage, five hourly time-series datasets covering a full
calendar year (8\,760~time steps) are assembled:
day-ahead electricity spot prices~$\lambda(t)$~[\euro/kWh],
solar irradiation~$G(t)$~[W/m\textsuperscript{2}] and ambient
temperature~$T_\mathrm{amb}(t)$ from a typical meteorological year~(TMY) file,
the building's thermal heat demand~$Q_\mathrm{dem}(t)$~[kW],
and its electrical demand~$P_\mathrm{dem}(t)$~[kW].
These datasets are described in detail in Section~\ref{sec:data_inputs}.

In the second stage the raw time series are normalised, validated, and
aligned to a common hourly resolution.
HP-specific pre-processing converts the ambient temperature record into hourly
COP and capacity-fraction arrays used as fixed parameters by the solver
(Section~\ref{sec:data_inputs}).

The third stage formulates and solves the MILP.
The objective function minimises the total annualised system cost,
comprising capital expenditure~(CAPEX) and operational expenditure~(OPEX):
\begin{equation}
    \min_{\mathbf{x},\,\mathbf{u}(t)}\;
    \mathrm{CAPEX}(\mathbf{x}) + \mathrm{OPEX}(\mathbf{u}(t))
    \label{eq:milp_obj}
\end{equation}
subject to the electricity and thermal energy balances,
state-of-charge bounds, component power limits,
binary on/off decisions, and grid capacity constraints,
enforced for every time step $t \in \{1,\ldots,8760\}$.
The complete component models, energy balances, and objective function are
developed in Sections~\ref{sec:component_models}--\ref{sec:objective_function};
the solver configuration is documented in Section~\ref{sec:solver_settings}.

The fourth and final stage returns the cost-optimal installed
capacities: BESS capacity~$C_\mathrm{bat}^*$~[kWh], HP thermal output~$Q_\mathrm{hp}^*$~[kW],
PV peak power~$P_\mathrm{pv}^*$~[kWp], and TES sizing variable.

    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{images/Energy_System_Schematic_final.png}
    \caption{
        Energy system schematic of the studied commercial building configuration,

        Dashed arrows indicate technologies to be replaced; solid orange arrows

    \label{fig:energy_system}

The energy system under study is depicted in Figure~\ref{fig:energy_system}.
The commercial building has a simultaneous electrical demand $P_\mathrm{dem}(t)$
and thermal heat demand $Q_\mathrm{dem}(t)$, both resolved at hourly resolution
over a full calendar year.
Thermal supply is currently provided by two gas boilers, which are designated
for replacement by the optimised system.

Four technologies constitute the candidate system.
On the electrical side, a PV array generates renewable electricity from incident
solar irradiation; a BESS enables temporal shifting of electrical energy through

On the thermal side, an HP converts electrical energy into heat; a TES tank

\subsubsection*{BDEW synthetic profile methodology}

Given the absence of reliable on-site metering, the thermal heat demand profile was instead constructed using the standardised BDEW (Bundesverband der Energie- und Wasserwirtschaft) standard load profile methodology for non-residential buildings, implemented via the Python \texttt{demandlib} library \cite{demandlib}.
This approach is routinely applied in energy system studies when sub-hourly measurement data are unavailable or unreliable, and has been extended to Belgium through the \textit{when2heat} project \cite{Ruhnau2019}.

The methodology proceeds in three stages: (i) computation of a smoothed \textit{reference temperature} from local weather data, (ii) mapping of that reference temperature to a daily demand factor through the BDEW sigmoid function, and (iii) disaggregation of the daily factor to hourly resolution using a BGW lookup table that accounts for weekday/weekend and temperature-bin variations.

\paragraph{Reference temperature.}
To account for thermal inertia in the building fabric, the BDEW standard does not use the instantaneous daily mean temperature directly.
Instead, a weighted reference temperature $T_\mathrm{ref}$ is formed as
\begin{equation}
    T_\mathrm{ref}(d) = 0.50\,T_\mathrm{daily}(d)
                      + 0.25\,T_\mathrm{daily}(d-1)
                      + 0.125\,T_\mathrm{daily}(d-2)
                      + 0.125\,T_\mathrm{daily}(d-3),
    \label{eq:T_ref}
\end{equation}
where $T_\mathrm{daily}(d)$ is the 24-hour mean outdoor temperature on day $d$.
This four-day exponentially-weighted average damps the demand response to short temperature excursions and better reflects the delayed thermal response of commercial buildings.

Hourly outdoor temperatures were obtained from the Open-Meteo historical archive API ($T_\mathrm{2m}$ field) at the site coordinates (Eeklo, Belgium: $51.18^{\circ}$N, $3.55^{\circ}$E).

\paragraph{Belgium heating threshold correction.}
The BDEW sigmoid parameters were originally calibrated for Germany, where the space-heating activation threshold is approximately 13.98°C.
Belgium's threshold, as tabulated in Kozarcanin et al.\ \cite{Kozarcanin2019} and adopted by when2heat, is 15.2°C.
To remove this systematic bias, the reference temperature series is uniformly shifted by
\begin{equation}
    \Delta T_\mathrm{BE} = 15.2 - 13.98 = +1.22\,\text{K}
    \label{eq:T_offset}
\end{equation}
before entering the sigmoid function.

\paragraph{Daily demand factor.}
The adjusted reference temperature is mapped to a dimensionless daily demand factor $h(d)$ through the BDEW sigmoid:
\begin{equation}
    h(d) = \frac{A}{1 + \left(\dfrac{B}{T_\mathrm{ref}(d) - 40}\right)^C} + D,
    \label{eq:bdew_sigmoid}
\end{equation}
where the parameters $A$, $B$, $C$, and $D$ are tabulated by the BDEW for each building type, windiness class, and building energy standard.
For this study the building type \textit{GKO} (Kaufhaus, i.e.\ retail/commercial shop) and wind class~1 (exposed, representative for the Belgian coastal climate) were selected, with building class~0 as required for non-residential building types.
The sigmoid saturates at low temperatures (high demand) and approaches zero for $T_\mathrm{ref} \gtrsim 15^\circ$C, reflecting the near-zero space-heating demand during summer.

\paragraph{Hourly disaggregation.}
Each day's demand factor $h(d)$ is distributed across 24 hours using the BGW hourly profile table.
This table provides dimensionless shape factors indexed by weekday (Monday through Sunday), hour of day (0--23), and temperature bin ($-15, -10, \ldots, 25, 30$°C).
Linear interpolation between the two nearest temperature bins is applied for each hour.
The product $h(d) \times f_\mathrm{BGW}(\text{weekday}, \text{hour}, T_\mathrm{ref}) \times 24$ yields a raw hourly profile that, when summed over the year, is normalised to unity and subsequently scaled to the known annual thermal energy demand.

\paragraph{Annual demand scaling.}
The M-accent building historically consumed approximately 260\,MWh/year for space heating, based on gas billing records.
This figure serves as the annual scaling constant; each normalised hourly profile value is multiplied by 260\,MWh to obtain the final hourly demand series $Q_\mathrm{dem}(t)$ in kWh.

\subsubsection*{Validation}

The generated BDEW profile was cross-validated against the historical gas consumption records for the seasons 2018/2019 and 2021/2022, and against the partial calorimetric measurement series for December 2025 -- March 2026 (used as a qualitative reference only, given the limitations above).
Monthly aggregates from the BDEW profile were found to be consistent with the gas records, confirming that the synthetic profile correctly reproduces the seasonal distribution of heating demand for this building type and climate.

\subsection{Electricity demand}
\label{subsec:elec_demand}

The electrical load profile $P_\mathrm{load}(t)$ [kW] is derived from measured
smart-meter data rather than a synthetic profile, ensuring the optimisation
reflects the actual consumption pattern of the M-accent building.

\subsubsection{Data source and preprocessing}

Quarter-hourly grid offtake and injection readings are exported from the Fluvius
digital meter for two EAN connections at the building.
The raw CSV files contain registers \textit{Afname Actief} (grid import) and
\textit{Injectie Actief} (grid export) in kWh per 15-minute interval.
A third meter records day/night split consumption for an auxiliary connection.
The three sources are combined into a single time series of total building
electricity demand:
\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{load}(t) = P_\mathrm{buy}(t) + P_\mathrm{PV}(t) - P_\mathrm{inj}(t),
    \label{eq:elec_demand}
\end{equation}
where $P_\mathrm{buy}$ is the total grid offtake across all meters,
$P_\mathrm{PV}$ is the gross on-site PV production, and $P_\mathrm{inj}$ is the
power injected back into the grid (stored in \texttt{Totaal\_verbruik\_kWh}).
Quarter-hourly kWh values are summed to hourly resolution; the resulting hourly
kWh equals the average kW over that hour.
Any gaps in the meter record are filled by linear time interpolation followed by
forward- and back-fill.

\subsubsection{Year selection}

The baseline model uses the 2025 meter record ($T = 8\,760$ hourly values).
The annual total consumption derived from the meter data is used directly as the
energy balance input to the MILP; no scaling or synthetic load shape is applied.

\subsubsection{Annual consumption}

The 2025 meter record yields a total annual electricity consumption of
approximately 100\,MWh (\texttt{Totaal\_verbruik\_kWh}: 100.5\,MWh), of which
78.4\,MWh was drawn from the grid ($P_\mathrm{buy}$), 37.7\,MWh was generated
by the existing PV system ($P_\mathrm{PV}$), 15.6\,MWh was injected back into
the grid ($P_\mathrm{inj}$), and the remaining 22.1\,MWh covered by on-site
PV self-consumption.
This places the building in Eurostat band\,IB (20--500\,MWh/yr), consistent with
the classification used for the price cross-validation in
Section~\ref{subsec:price_data}.

\subsection{Day-ahead electricity prices}
\label{subsec:price_data}

\subsubsection{Price data source}

The hourly day-ahead electricity price series $\lambda_\mathrm{EPEX}(t)$ is retrieved from the
ENTSO-E Transparency Platform via the \texttt{entsoe-py} Python client \cite{entsoe-py}
for the Belgian bidding zone (\texttt{BE}).
Raw quarter-hourly prices in \euro/MWh are resampled to hourly means and converted to
\euro/kWh, yielding an 8\,760-element array aligned with the model's time steps.
The baseline optimisation uses the 2025 price series; a uniform \texttt{price\_multiplier}
is applied in the sensitivity analysis to explore a range of future price levels
(see Table~\ref{tab:sens_params}).

\subsubsection{Consumer tariff structure}

Spot prices are converted to consumer buy and sell prices using the Ecopower
\textit{Dynamische burgerstroom} tariff \cite{Ecopower2026tariff} (valid from
1\,January\,2026, shown in Appendix~\ref{appendix:tariefkaart}) and the Fluvius
Imewo distribution tariffs for the Eeklo grid area.
The buy price is:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_\mathrm{buy}(t) = 0.00102\,\lambda_\mathrm{EPEX}(t) + c_\mathrm{markup},
    \label{eq:buy_price}
\end{equation}
where $c_\mathrm{markup}$ is the sum of all fixed per-kWh surcharges listed in
Table~\ref{tab:price_components}.
The sell price for PV export is:
\begin{equation}
    \lambda_\mathrm{sell}(t) = 0.00098\,\lambda_\mathrm{EPEX}(t) - c_\mathrm{sell},
    \label{eq:sell_price}
\end{equation}
where $c_\mathrm{sell}$ aggregates the fixed feed-in offset and the Fluvius Imewo
injection tariff (see Table~\ref{tab:price_components}).
All operational cost terms carry 21\,\% VAT\@.

\subsubsection{Capacity network tariff}

The Fluvius Imewo capacity tariff is billed monthly on the highest 15-minute average
grid-import peak recorded in that month.
The annualised rate of 60\,\euro/kW/year is used in the model.
This tariff creates a direct financial incentive for the MILP optimiser to reduce peak
grid imports, motivating investment in BESS and TES for peak shaving
(see Eqs.~\eqref{eq:peak_track}--\eqref{eq:opex_cap} in
Section~\ref{subsec:opex} for the MILP formulation).

\subsubsection{Tariff validation}

The tariff structure was validated against the 2023 ENTSO-E Belgian day-ahead price
series (mean 97.27\,\euro/MWh).
Applying Equation~\eqref{eq:buy_price} yields a mean all-in buy price of
0.1837\,\euro/kWh excluding VAT, or 0.222\,\euro/kWh including 21\,\% VAT.
Eurostat reports 0.253\,\euro/kWh for the comparable Belgian non-household band in
H1\,2023~\cite{Eurostat_nrg_pc_205}, a difference of $-12.1$\,\%.
This is expected: the Eurostat figure averages across all suppliers and tariff types,
including fixed-price contracts that still reflected elevated 2022 crisis prices,
whereas the Ecopower dynamic product tracked the sharply lower 2023 spot prices.

For PV export, the mean sell price was 0.0786\,\euro/kWh (0.095\,\euro/kWh incl.\ VAT),
with a minimum of $-0.163$\,\euro/kWh during periods of negative spot prices.
In 7.2\,\% of all hours (634\,h/year) the sell price turned negative, meaning export
was economically penalised; the MILP can exploit this by curtailing injection or
shifting load to those hours via storage.

\subsection{Solar irradiance pre-processing}
\label{subsec:pv_irradiance}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The pre-processing step produces a normalised hourly specific yield:
\begin{equation}
    I_\mathrm{sol}(t) \quad [\mathrm{kW/kW_p}], \qquad t = 1, \ldots, 8760,
    \label{eq:pv_sol_output}
\end{equation}
which is stored as a fixed parameter array before the optimisation begins,
keeping every MILP constraint in which it appears linear.

\subsubsection*{Why not PVGIS}

The European Commission's PVGIS API~\cite{Huld2012PVGIS} was the natural first
candidate: it provides a free, well-validated hourly AC power series from satellite
climatology and has been shown to achieve the lowest annual error among 14 widely used
PV simulation tools~\cite{Milosavljevic2022PVtools}.
However, PVGIS currently covers only up to 2023, whereas the scenario analysis
in this thesis requires solar profiles for 2024 and 2025.
This alignment is essential: because large-scale PV deployment across Europe has
substantially increased the frequency of near-zero and negative day-ahead electricity
prices in recent years, using a mismatched solar-year would distort the co-optimisation
of PV sizing and price-driven dispatch.
PVGIS was therefore set aside in favour of a pipeline that can deliver up-to-date data
for any target year.
Figure~\ref{fig:low_price_hours} illustrates the trend: the number of hours per year
with a Belgian day-ahead spot price below 30\,\euro/MWh has risen sharply since 2021,
confirming that year-specific price alignment is material to the optimisation results.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{images/low_price_hours.png}
    \caption{Number of hours per year with a Belgian day-ahead spot price below
             30\,\euro/MWh (ENTSO-E transparency platform, Belgium bidding zone).
             The pronounced increase from 2021 onwards reflects the rapid growth in
             renewable generation capacity across the Central Western Europe price zone.}
    \label{fig:low_price_hours}
\end{figure}

\subsubsection*{Open-Meteo + pvlib pipeline}

Hourly irradiance components (GHI, DNI, DHI), ambient temperature, and 10\,m wind speed
are retrieved from the Open-Meteo historical archive API for the building site
($51.18^{\circ}$N, $3.55^{\circ}$E).
Open-Meteo is a free, open-source reanalysis product that combines ERA5 and
high-resolution numerical weather prediction (NWP) model output and requires no API key,
making it straightforward to obtain consistent multi-year time series.
The archive provides hourly data at roughly 5--11\,km spatial resolution and is
available for any historical year up to the present.

Plane-of-array (POA) irradiance is then computed locally with \texttt{pvlib} using the
Hay--Davies transposition model~\cite{Hay1980} for a fixed tilt of $35^{\circ}$
(the widely cited energy-yield optimum for the Belgian latitude of $51^{\circ}$\,N~\cite{Jacobson2018tilt},
feasible because the building has a flat roof) and an azimuth of $190^{\circ}$
(10$^{\circ}$ west of south), read from a Google Maps satellite view of the M-accent roof.
The Hay--Davies model computes the total plane-of-array irradiance as the sum of three
components:
\begin{equation}
    G_\mathrm{POA}(t) = E_\mathrm{beam}(t) + E_\mathrm{diff}(t) + E_\mathrm{refl}(t).
    \label{eq:poa_total}
\end{equation}
The beam component is projected onto the tilted surface using the ratio $R_b$ of the
cosine of the angle of incidence $\theta_\mathrm{AOI}$ to the cosine of the solar
zenith angle $Z$:
\begin{equation}
    E_\mathrm{beam}(t) = \mathrm{DNI}(t)\cdot R_b(t),
    \qquad R_b = \frac{\cos\theta_\mathrm{AOI}}{\cos Z}.
\end{equation}
The sky diffuse component is split into a circumsolar fraction (treated as originating
from the solar disk) and an isotropic background integrated over the visible sky dome,
weighted by the anisotropy index $A_i = \mathrm{DNI}/E_a$, where $E_a$ is the
extraterrestrial irradiance, i.e.\ the solar irradiance incident on a surface
perpendicular to the sun's rays at the top of the atmosphere, which varies
seasonally as the Earth--Sun distance changes (from roughly
$1\,322\,\mathrm{W\,m}^{-2}$ in July to $1\,413\,\mathrm{W\,m}^{-2}$ in January)
and is computed by \texttt{pvlib} following Spencer~(1971):
\begin{equation}
    E_\mathrm{diff}(t) = \mathrm{DHI}(t)\left[
        A_i(t)\,R_b(t)
        + \bigl(1 - A_i(t)\bigr)\frac{1+\cos\beta}{2}
    \right].
    \label{eq:haydavies_diff}
\end{equation}
Ground-reflected irradiance is added using a fixed albedo $\rho$:
\begin{equation}
    E_\mathrm{refl}(t) = \mathrm{GHI}(t)\cdot\rho\cdot\frac{1-\cos\beta}{2}.
\end{equation}
When $A_i\to0$ (overcast sky, no direct beam) the diffuse term reduces to the
isotropic model; when $A_i\to1$ all diffuse is concentrated around the sun.
This anisotropic treatment improves accuracy over the pure isotropic model at the cost
of one additional input (DNI), which Open-Meteo provides directly.
Solar positions are evaluated at the midpoint of each hour to reduce numerical
integration error.
The normalised specific yield is obtained from:
\begin{equation}
    I_\mathrm{sol}(t) = \frac{G_\mathrm{POA}(t)}{1000} \cdot \eta_\mathrm{temp}(t) \cdot (1 - \ell),
\end{equation}
where $G_\mathrm{POA}(t)$ is the plane-of-array irradiance in W\,m$^{-2}$,
$\eta_\mathrm{temp}(t)$ is the PVWatts temperature correction factor, and
$\ell$ is a fixed loss factor covering wiring, shading, soiling, and DC-to-AC
conversion.
The temperature correction follows the PVWatts model:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_\mathrm{temp}(t) = 1 + \gamma_\mathrm{pdc}\,\bigl(T_\mathrm{cell}(t) - T_\mathrm{ref}\bigr),
    \quad \gamma_\mathrm{pdc} = -0.004\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}^{-1},
    \quad T_\mathrm{ref} = 25\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C},
    \label{eq:pv_temp_correction}
\end{equation}
where the cell temperature $T_\mathrm{cell}(t)$ is pre-computed in the
pre-processing pipeline using the Faiman~\cite{Faiman2008} model:
\begin{equation}
    T_\mathrm{cell}(t) = T_\mathrm{amb}(t)
        + \frac{G_\mathrm{POA}(t)}{u_0 + u_1\,v_\mathrm{wind}(t)},
    \quad u_0 = 25\,\mathrm{W\,m^{-2}\,K^{-1}},\;
           u_1 = 6.84\,\mathrm{W\,s\,m^{-3}\,K^{-1}},
    \label{eq:faiman}
\end{equation}
with $T_\mathrm{amb}(t)$ and $v_\mathrm{wind}(t)$ (10\,m wind speed) both supplied
by the Open-Meteo fetch.
Because $T_\mathrm{cell}(t)$ is computed entirely from known climate inputs before
the optimisation runs, $\eta_\mathrm{temp}(t)$ is a fixed scalar at each time step,
keeping the constraint $P_\mathrm{PV}(t) = C_\mathrm{PV}\cdot I_\mathrm{sol}(t)$
linear in the sizing variable $C_\mathrm{PV}$.

\subsubsection*{Loss calibration against 2025 measured data}

The loss factor $\ell$ was calibrated against the 15-minute sub-meter production
data of the existing 34.63\,kW$_\mathrm{p}$ rooftop system for the full year 2025.
With the updated PVWatts\,+\,Faiman model (Eqs.~\eqref{eq:pv_temp_correction}
and~\eqref{eq:faiman}), temperature derating is now captured explicitly by
$\eta_\mathrm{temp}(t)$, so $\ell$ covers only the remaining fixed losses:
wiring resistance, inter-row shading, soiling, and DC-to-AC inverter losses.
Irradiance components (GHI, DNI, DHI), ambient temperature, and 10\,m wind speed
were obtained from the Open-Meteo historical archive for the site coordinates.
The factor was found by bisection so that the simulated annual total matched
the measured 2025 total of 37\,747\,kWh, yielding $\ell = 28.14\,\%$.
The NREL PVWatts\,v5 documentation~\cite{Dobos2014PVWatts} identifies the
principal fixed-loss categories for grid-connected rooftop systems and quotes
the following typical ranges: DC wiring resistance ($1$--$3\,\%$), soiling
($0$--$5\,\%$, lower in climates with frequent rainfall such as Belgium), inter-row
shading ($0$--$5\,\%$), and DC-to-AC inverter losses ($2$--$4\,\%$), giving an
aggregate physical loss budget of roughly $5$--$17\,\%$.
The calibrated $\ell = 28.14\,\%$ exceeds this physical range primarily because the
fixed inter-row shading term in the model does not capture the strongly seasonal
nature of near-horizon shading at this latitude.
At $51^{\circ}$\,N the solar elevation at solar noon falls below $20^{\circ}$ in
December, meaning that surrounding buildings and rooftop obstacles that cast no shadow
in summer can block a significant fraction of the incoming beam in winter.
This effect manifests as a pronounced positive bias in the out-of-sample validation
(up to $+80\,\%$ in December; see Section below), and $\ell$ absorbs its
annual-average impact so that the total energy balance remains correct over the full year.

\subsubsection*{Out-of-sample validation against 2024 measured data}

To confirm that the calibrated pipeline generalises beyond the year used for fitting,
the loss-corrected model is applied to 2024, a fully independent year, and
compared against the corresponding 15-minute sub-meter measurements
(Figures~\ref{fig:openmeteo_daily_2024} and~\ref{fig:openmeteo_monthly_2024}).

Over the full year 2024 the model simulates 32.31\,MWh against a measured total of
31.78\,MWh, an annual overestimation of $+1.7\,\%$.
The goodness of fit is quantified by two standard metrics computed from the $N = 8782$
aligned hourly observations $(\hat{P}_t,\, P_t^\mathrm{meas})$:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{t=1}^{N}\!\left(\hat{P}_t - P_t^\mathrm{meas}\right)^2},
    \label{eq:rmse}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
    R^2 = 1 - \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{t=1}^{N}\!\left(\hat{P}_t - P_t^\mathrm{meas}\right)^2}
                   {\displaystyle\sum_{t=1}^{N}\!\left(P_t^\mathrm{meas} - \bar{P}^\mathrm{meas}\right)^2},
    \label{eq:r2}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\hat{P}_t$ is the simulated hourly power, $P_t^\mathrm{meas}$ the
measured hourly power, and $\bar{P}^\mathrm{meas}$ their mean.
Applying these to the 2024 data yields $R^2 = 0.789$ and
$\mathrm{RMSE} = 2.86\,\mathrm{kW}$.
$R^2$ measures how much of the variance in the measured hourly output is explained by
the model: a value close to one means the simulated and measured profiles rise and fall
together, the model captures the right days as sunny or overcast, and the right hours
as peak or off-peak.
The RMSE quantifies the typical hour-by-hour deviation in absolute power terms: at
$2.86\,\mathrm{kW}$ on a $34.63\,\mathrm{kW_p}$ system this corresponds to roughly
$8\,\%$ of peak capacity, reflecting the coarser spatial resolution of the Open-Meteo
reanalysis relative to site-specific satellite products.

Monthly breakdown (Figure~\ref{fig:openmeteo_monthly_2024}) reveals a pronounced
seasonal bias pattern: summer months (April--August) carry a negative bias of
$1$--$12\,\%$ (model underestimates), while winter months show a strong positive
overestimation of up to $+80\,\%$ in December and November.
This seasonal asymmetry is a known characteristic of ERA5-based reanalysis products at
northern latitudes, where low solar elevation angles and frequent cloud and fog events
are difficult to reproduce at coarse spatial resolution.
Because the winter months contribute only a small fraction of the annual yield, the
annual error remains low ($+1.7\,\%$), and the bias has a limited effect on the
optimisation outcomes.

Figure~\ref{fig:openmeteo_daily_profiles_2024} overlays the simulated and measured intraday
power profiles for one representative day per month.
In the summer months the hourly Open-Meteo profile tracks the smooth bell-shaped generation
curve reasonably well.
In the winter months the model correctly identifies the midday generation window but
overestimates the magnitude, consistent with the positive winter bias noted above.

\subsection{HP time-series pre-processing}
\label{sub:hthp_preprocess}
% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The HP model requires two ambient-temperature-dependent parameter arrays that must
be computed from climate data before the optimisation begins: the hourly COP time
series and the hourly capacity-fraction time series.
Both are derived from the same ambient temperature vector $T_\mathrm{amb}(t)$ supplied
by the Open-Meteo\,+\,pvlib pipeline (Section~\ref{subsec:pv_irradiance}) and are passed
to the model as fixed scalar parameters, keeping every MILP constraint linear.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Pre-processing step 1: Lorentz COP time series}
\label{sub:hthp_cop_preprocess}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

As established in Section~\ref{sub:lorentz_model}, the Lorentz model expresses the ideal COP
of a heat pump in terms of the log-mean temperatures on the condenser (sink) side and the
evaporator (source) side.
With a fixed supply temperature $T_\mathrm{sup}$ [$^{\circ}$C] and fixed return temperature
$T_\mathrm{ret}$ [$^{\circ}$C], the log-mean condenser temperature is a constant.
For the M-accent system, $T_\mathrm{sup} = 60\,^{\circ}$C is taken directly from the
measured heat demand data (Section~\ref{subsec:thermal_demand}), which shows a consistent
supply temperature of 60\,°C.
The return temperature is then $T_\mathrm{ret} = T_\mathrm{sup} - \Delta T_\mathrm{HC}
= 60 - 20 = 40\,^{\circ}$C, where $\Delta T_\mathrm{HC} = 20\,\mathrm{K}$ is the
design temperature drop across the radiators as specified in the M-accent P\&ID.
These values yield $T_\mathrm{h,lm} \approx 323\,\mathrm{K}$:

\begin{equation}
    T_{\mathrm{h,lm}} =
    \frac{T_\mathrm{sup} - T_\mathrm{ret}}
         {\ln\!\left(\dfrac{T_\mathrm{sup}+273.15}{T_\mathrm{ret}+273.15}\right)}
    \quad [\mathrm{K}].
    \label{eq:T_hlm}
\end{equation}

The heat source is outdoor air at the hourly ambient temperature $T_\mathrm{amb}(t)$
[$^{\circ}$C], converted to Kelvin as $T_\mathrm{src}(t) = T_\mathrm{amb}(t)+273.15$~[K].
The ideal Lorentz COP at each hour is therefore:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{COP}_\mathrm{Lorenz}(t)
    = \frac{T_{\mathrm{h,lm}}}{T_{\mathrm{h,lm}} - T_\mathrm{src}(t)}.
    \label{eq:cop_lorenz_t}
\end{equation}

The actual COP is obtained by scaling with the Lorentz efficiency factor
$\eta_\mathrm{HP}$, calibrated from manufacturer performance data
(Appendix~\ref{appendix:datasheet_hthp}) at the standard EN\,14511 reference operating
point A7/W40--45: ambient air at $T_\mathrm{amb} = 7\,^{\circ}$C, condenser return at
$T_\mathrm{ret} = 40\,^{\circ}$C, and condenser supply at $T_\mathrm{sup} = 45\,^{\circ}$C:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{COP}(t) = \eta_\mathrm{HP} \cdot \mathrm{COP}_\mathrm{Lorenz}(t),
    \qquad \eta_\mathrm{HP} = 0.361.
    \label{eq:cop_actual_t}
\end{equation}

\noindent Because both $T_\mathrm{h,lm}$ and $\eta_\mathrm{HP}$ are constants, and
$T_\mathrm{amb}(t)$ is a known input time series, $\mathrm{COP}(t)$ is fully determined
before the optimisation begins.
It is passed to the model as a \emph{scalar parameter} at each time step, making every
constraint in which it appears linear.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Pre-processing step 2: capacity-fraction linear regression}
\label{sub:hthp_capfrac}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

At low ambient temperatures an air-source heat pump cannot deliver its rated thermal output:
the refrigerant mass-flow rate decreases as the evaporator pressure drops.
This effect is \emph{separate} from the COP variation and must be modelled independently as
a reduction in the \emph{available} capacity.

A \textbf{linear regression} is fitted to the heating-capacity data extracted from the
Daikin EWYE-CZ manufacturer simulation at full load and a fixed condenser leaving water
temperature of $55\,^{\circ}$C (eight points from $-15\,^{\circ}$C to $+7\,^{\circ}$C
plus one high-temperature point at $+20\,^{\circ}$C).
The regression is forced through the reference point $(T_\mathrm{ref} = 7\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C},\;
\kappa = 1.0)$, yielding:

\begin{equation}
    \kappa(t) = 1 + k \bigl(T_\mathrm{amb}(t) - T_\mathrm{ref}\bigr),
    \quad k = 0.02106\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}^{-1},\quad T_\mathrm{ref} = 7\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C},
    \label{eq:capfrac}
\end{equation}

\noindent clipped to the manufacturer operating envelope:
$\kappa(t) \in [0.537,\; 1.274]$ (corresponding to
$T_\mathrm{amb} \in [-15\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C},\; +20\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}]$).
The fit achieves $R^2 = 0.997$, meaning the linear model explains 99.7\,\% of the
variance in the manufacturer capacity data across the operating temperature range,
confirming that the capacity derating is well described by a linear function of ambient
temperature over the relevant operating envelope.
Like $\mathrm{COP}(t)$, the array $\kappa(t)$ is pre-computed and enters the MILP
as a fixed coefficient.

\noindent \textbf{Physical interpretation.}
A value $\kappa < 1$ means the unit can deliver less than its nameplate output at cold
conditions; a value $\kappa > 1$ means it can slightly exceed nameplate at mild conditions.
The effective weather-derated capacity at any hour is therefore
$Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*} \cdot \kappa(t)$ [kW].

    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/heat_pump_performance.png}
    \caption{Heat pump performance characteristics used for the HP model. Left: fitted capacity-fraction vs ambient temperature from manufacturer simulation data, which yields the slope $k = 0.02106\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}^{-1}$ and the clipped operating envelope. Right: part-load performance illustrating the degradation coefficient $C_d$ used to represent fixed auxiliary electrical overhead at part-load.}
    \label{fig:hthp_performance}
\end{figure}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{High-temperature heat pump (HP)}
\label{sec:hthp_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Of the COP modeling approaches surveyed in Section~\ref{sub:milp_advantages}, the temperature-dependent Lorentz-calibrated pre-computed time series is adopted, as described in Section~\ref{sub:lorentz_model}.
The manufacturer efficiency factor is calibrated to $\eta_\mathrm{HP} = 0.361$ from the Daikin EWYE-CZ datasheet (Appendix~\ref{appendix:datasheet_hthp}).

The HP is the primary source of thermal energy in the system,
converting electrical power into heat at supply temperatures up to $75\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$,
making it suitable for the existing radiator circuit of the M-accent building without requiring
hydronic renovation.
The specific unit modelled is the Daikin EWYE-CZ air-to-water heat pump; its full manufacturer
datasheet is shown in Appendix~\ref{appendix:datasheet_hthp}.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Governing model equations}
\label{sub:hthp_equations}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

Three equations govern the HP model.
The first two capture ambient-temperature-dependent effects and are fully derived in
Section~\ref{sub:hthp_preprocess}; they are reproduced here for reference.
The thermodynamic efficiency is described by the Lorentz-calibrated COP
(Eq.~\eqref{eq:cop_actual_t}):

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{COP}(t) = \eta_\mathrm{HP} \cdot \mathrm{COP}_\mathrm{Lorenz}(t),
    \qquad \eta_\mathrm{HP} = 0.361. \tag{\ref{eq:cop_actual_t}}
\end{equation}

\noindent The weather-dependent capacity derating is described by the linear regression
fit to manufacturer data (Eq.~\eqref{eq:capfrac}):

\begin{equation}
    \kappa(t) = 1 + k\bigl(T_\mathrm{amb}(t) - T_\mathrm{ref}\bigr),
    \quad k = 0.02106\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}^{-1}, \tag{\ref{eq:capfrac}}
\end{equation}

\noindent so that the available thermal capacity at time step $t$ is
$Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}\cdot\kappa(t)$ [kW$_\mathrm{th}$].
The third equation links electrical power consumption to thermal output.
The heat pump is inverter-driven, so fixed auxiliary consumers (circulation pump,
controls, fans) are active whenever it runs, independently of compressor modulation.
Following the EN~14511 part-load degradation formalism
(Section~\ref{sub:part_load_modeling}), the electrical draw is split into a fixed
fraction and a variable fraction:

\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{el,HP}(t)\cdot\mathrm{COP}(t)
    = C_\mathrm{d}\,\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)\cdot\kappa(t)
      + \bigl(1-C_\mathrm{d}\bigr)\,Q_\mathrm{HP}(t),
    \quad C_\mathrm{d} = 0.10.
    \label{eq:hthp_power}
\end{equation}

\noindent At full load this reduces to the ideal relation
$P_\mathrm{el,HP} = Q_\mathrm{HP}/\mathrm{COP}$; at part load the $C_\mathrm{d}$
term adds a modest overhead that slightly reduces the effective part-load COP.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Decision variables}
\label{sub:hthp_vars}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

\begin{itemize}

    \item $y_\mathrm{HP} \in \{0,1\}$: binary installation decision (1 if the heat pump
          is included in the system, 0 otherwise).
          This variable is required to correctly model the fixed-cost component of the
          annualised capital expenditure: the total HP cost is split into a fixed term
          incurred only if the technology is installed and a capacity-proportional term,
          \begin{equation}
              C_\mathrm{HP,annual} = \alpha_\mathrm{HP,fixed}\,y_\mathrm{HP}
                                   + \alpha_\mathrm{HP,var}\,Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*},
              \label{eq:hthp_capex}
          \end{equation}
          where $\alpha_\mathrm{HP,fixed}$ and $\alpha_\mathrm{HP,var}$ are derived
          from the linear-affine CAPEX fit of Section~\ref{sub:capex_model},
          Eq.~\eqref{eq:capex_affine}.
          Without $y_\mathrm{HP}$, the fixed cost would either always be charged or
          always be omitted regardless of whether the solver installs the unit.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*} \in [0,\; Q_\mathrm{hp,max}^{*}]$ [kW$_\mathrm{th}$]:
          continuous sizing variable (rated thermal output at reference conditions).

    \item $u_\mathrm{HP}(t) \in \{0,1\}$, $t=1,\ldots,8\,760$: binary on/off
          commitment.
          Without this variable the minimum-load constraint would read
          $Q_\mathrm{HP}(t) \geq f_\mathrm{min}\,Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}\,\kappa(t)$,
          a strictly positive lower bound for any non-zero sized unit that forces
          the heat pump on at every time step.
          Likewise, the fixed auxiliary overhead in Eq.~\eqref{eq:hthp_power} would
          remain active at idle, spuriously drawing electricity.
          The binary resolves both issues: when $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)=0$ the effective
          capacity is zero, making all bounds non-binding.
          Introducing 8\,760 per-timestep binaries carries a significant computational
          price, unlike the five technology-selection binaries
          ($y_\mathrm{PV}$, $y_\mathrm{bat}$, $y_\mathrm{HP}$, $y_\mathrm{ltes}$,
          $y_\mathrm{wtes}$), which add negligible combinatorial complexity, each
          additional operational binary can exponentially increase the branch-and-bound
          search tree and this block dominates solver cost.

    \item $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) \geq 0$ [kW$_\mathrm{th}$]: auxiliary continuous
          variable that replaces the bilinear product
          $Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}\cdot u_\mathrm{HP}(t)$.
          Because $Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$ is continuous and $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)$ is binary,
          their product cannot appear directly in an MILP.
          The big-$M$ constraints of Section~\ref{sub:hthp_bigm} force
          $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) = Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$ when on and
          $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) = 0$ when off, replacing the product exactly.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{HP}(t) \geq 0$ [kW$_\mathrm{th}$]: actual thermal output
          delivered at time step $t$.

    \item $P_\mathrm{el,HP}(t) \geq 0$ [kW$_\mathrm{el}$]: electrical power consumed
          at time step $t$.

\end{itemize}

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{On/off linearisation (big-$M$)}
\label{sub:hthp_bigm}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The three linear constraints that force $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)$ to equal
$Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$ when $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)=1$ and zero when $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)=0$
are:

\begin{align}
    \tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) &\leq Q_\mathrm{hp,max}^{*}\, u_\mathrm{HP}(t)
        \label{eq:hthp_bigm1} \\
    \tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) &\leq Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}
        \label{eq:hthp_bigm2} \\
    \tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) &\geq Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*} - Q_\mathrm{hp,max}^{*}\,
        \bigl(1 - u_\mathrm{HP}(t)\bigr).
        \label{eq:hthp_bigm3}
\end{align}

\noindent When $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)=0$, constraint \eqref{eq:hthp_bigm1} gives
$\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) \leq 0$, which combined with the non-negativity declaration
$\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t) \geq 0$ forces $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)=0$ exactly.
Without the non-negativity bound, equation~\eqref{eq:hthp_bigm1} alone would only
provide an upper bound and could not prevent $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)$ from becoming
negative.
When $u_\mathrm{HP}(t)=1$, constraints \eqref{eq:hthp_bigm2} and \eqref{eq:hthp_bigm3}
together force $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)=Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{MILP constraints}
\label{sub:hthp_constraints}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

Three operational constraints bound $Q_\mathrm{HP}(t)$ and $P_\mathrm{el,HP}(t)$.
The thermal output is bounded from above by the weather-derated capacity and from
below by the minimum load fraction $f_\mathrm{min}$ (set to $0.222$ from manufacturer
data):

\begin{align}
    Q_\mathrm{HP}(t) &\leq \tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)\cdot\kappa(t)
        \label{eq:hthp_qmax} \\
    Q_\mathrm{HP}(t) &\geq f_\mathrm{min}\,\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)\cdot\kappa(t).
        \label{eq:hthp_qmin}
\end{align}

\noindent The lower bound prevents unrealistically shallow part-load operation.
When the unit is off, $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)=0$ collapses both bounds to
$Q_\mathrm{HP}(t)=0$ exactly.
The electrical power consumption is governed by Eq.~\eqref{eq:hthp_power},
repeated here as a constraint:

\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{el,HP}(t)\cdot\mathrm{COP}(t)
    = C_\mathrm{d}\,\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)\cdot\kappa(t)
      + \bigl(1-C_\mathrm{d}\bigr)\,Q_\mathrm{HP}(t). \tag{\ref{eq:hthp_power}}
\end{equation}

\noindent Since $\mathrm{COP}(t)$ and $\kappa(t)$ are fixed scalars, all three
constraints are linear in the decision variables.
Using $\tilde{Q}_\mathrm{hp}(t)$ rather than $Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$ in the overhead
term ensures that $P_\mathrm{el,HP}(t)=0$ when the unit is off; had $Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}$
appeared instead, the fixed term $C_\mathrm{d}\,Q_\mathrm{hp}^{*}\cdot\kappa(t)$
would draw electricity at every idle step.

\subsection{Photovoltaic (PV) array}
\label{sec:pv_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Of the PV output calculation approaches reviewed in Section~\ref{sub:lit_pv_overview}, the model-based approach using the Open-Meteo\,+\,pvlib processing pipeline is adopted (Section~\ref{subsec:pv_irradiance}), as it enables the tool to be applied to any location from standard meteorological data without requiring on-site measurements.

The PV array provides on-site renewable electricity, directly offsetting grid imports and
supplying the HP, BESS, and building loads.
Its MILP representation is intentionally simple: all complex photovoltaic physics
(plane-of-array irradiance, cell temperature derating, DC-to-AC inverter losses,
soiling, and wiring losses) are handled entirely in a pre-processing step using the
Open-Meteo\,+\,pvlib pipeline (see Section~\ref{subsec:pv_irradiance}), so only a single
linear constraint is needed inside the optimiser.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Governing model equations}
\label{sub:pv_equations}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

One equation governs the PV model.
The AC power injected by the array at each time step is the product of the
pre-computed normalised specific yield $I_\mathrm{sol}(t)$ [kW/kW$_\mathrm{p}$]
and the installed peak capacity $C_\mathrm{PV}$ [kW$_\mathrm{p}$]:

\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{PV}(t) = I_\mathrm{sol}(t)\cdot C_\mathrm{PV},
    \qquad \forall\, t.
    \label{eq:pv_power}
\end{equation}

\noindent Because $I_\mathrm{sol}(t)$ is a fixed scalar at every time step,
the constraint is linear in the single sizing variable $C_\mathrm{PV}$.
$P_\mathrm{PV}(t)$ enters the electrical energy balance
(Section~\ref{sec:energy_balance}) as a generation term.

\subsubsection{MILP constraints}
\label{sub:pv_constraints}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

Three constraints govern the PV block.
The technology-selection constraint enforces consistency between the binary and the
sizing variable, ensuring no capacity is installed when $y_\mathrm{PV}=0$:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{PV} \leq C_\mathrm{PV,max}\, y_\mathrm{PV}.
    \label{eq:pv_select}
\end{equation}

impossible arbitrage), the power sold to the grid is capped at the sum of local
generation and battery discharge:
\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{sell}(t) \leq P_\mathrm{PV}(t) + P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t),
    \qquad \forall\, t.
    \label{eq:pv_sellcap}
\end{equation}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Battery energy storage system (BESS)}
\label{sec:bess_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The BESS model follows the standard SoC energy balance formulation reviewed in Section~\ref{sub:lit_bess_overview}, with symmetric one-way efficiencies $\eta_\mathrm{ch} = \eta_\mathrm{dis} = 0.95$ sourced from the BYD/SolarEdge datasheets and self-discharge neglected over hourly time steps \cite{Gabrielli2018Electrochemical}.

The BESS stores surplus PV generation for later use and performs price-arbitrage by
charging during cheap off-peak hours and discharging during expensive peak hours.
It also provides peak-shaving services that directly reduce the monthly capacity
network tariff $c_\mathrm{cap}$.
The electrochemical physics (cell voltage, temperature derating, cycle ageing) are
abstracted into two one-way efficiencies and a fixed C-rate, keeping the formulation
fully linear.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Governing model equations}
\label{sub:bess_equations}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The central equation of the BESS model is the state-of-charge (SoC) energy
balance, which links the storage level at each hour to the charge and discharge
power flows:
\begin{equation}
    E_\mathrm{bat}(t) = E_\mathrm{bat}(t-1)
        + \eta_\mathrm{ch}\,P_\mathrm{bat,ch}(t)\,\Delta t
        - \frac{1}{\eta_\mathrm{dis}}\,P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t)\,\Delta t,
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:bess_soc_dyn}
\end{equation}
The one-way losses are applied asymmetrically: the charging efficiency
$\eta_\mathrm{ch}$ reduces the energy actually stored per kWh injected, while
the discharge efficiency $\eta_\mathrm{dis}$ increases the energy that must be
drawn from storage to deliver one kWh to the bus.
Both coefficients are fixed scalars, so every term in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:bess_soc_dyn} is linear in the MILP variables.

The annualised BESS investment cost follows the same linear-affine structure
used for all other technologies (Section~\ref{sub:capex_model}):
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{bat,annual}
        = \alpha_\mathrm{bat,fixed}\,y_\mathrm{bat}
        + \alpha_\mathrm{bat,var}\,C_\mathrm{bat},
    \label{eq:bess_capex}
\end{equation}
where $y_\mathrm{bat}$ is the installation binary introduced below.
Without $y_\mathrm{bat}$, a solver that sets $C_\mathrm{bat} = 0$ would still
incur the fixed cost term, incorrectly penalising the no-BESS outcome.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Fixed parameters and inputs}
\label{sub:bess_params}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

All scalar coefficients embedded in the BESS constraints are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:bess_inputs}.
Because every coefficient is a fixed number known before optimisation, all
constraints remain linear in the decision variables.

\begin{table}[H]
\centering

\label{tab:bess_inputs}
\begin{tabular}{lllp{4.8cm}}
\hline
\textbf{Symbol} & \textbf{Value} & \textbf{Source} & \textbf{Role in model} \\
\hline
$\eta_\mathrm{ch}$ & 0.95 & BYD/SolarEdge datasheets & Charging efficiency \\
$\eta_\mathrm{dis}$ & 0.95 & BYD/SolarEdge datasheets & Discharge efficiency \\
$r_\mathrm{C}$ & 0.5\,kW/kWh & Manufacturer C-rate spec & Max charge/discharge rate \\

\hline

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Decision variables}
\label{sub:bess_vars}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

\begin{itemize}

    \item $y_\mathrm{bat} \in \{0,1\}$: binary installation decision (1 if a BESS
          is included in the system, 0 otherwise).
          Required to correctly model the fixed-cost component of the annualised
          CAPEX in Eq.~\eqref{eq:bess_capex}: without it, a zero-capacity solution
          would still incur the fixed installation cost.

    \item $C_\mathrm{bat} \in [0,\; C_\mathrm{bat,max}]$ [kWh]: continuous sizing
          variable representing the usable energy capacity.
          No separate rated-power variable is introduced; charge and discharge
          powers are instead bounded by a fixed C-rate relative to this capacity.

    \item $P_\mathrm{bat,ch}(t) \geq 0$ [kW]: charge power drawn from the AC bus
          at each hour.

    \item $P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t) \geq 0$ [kW]: discharge power delivered to the
          AC bus at each hour.

    \item $E_\mathrm{bat}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh]: state of charge at each hour,
          determined by the SoC dynamics Eq.~\eqref{eq:bess_soc_dyn}.

\end{itemize}

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{MILP constraints}
\label{sub:bess_constraints}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The technology-selection constraint couples the binary installation decision to
the sizing variable, forcing zero capacity when the BESS is not selected:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{bat} \leq C_\mathrm{bat,max}\,y_\mathrm{bat}.
    \label{eq:bat_binary}
\end{equation}

The SoC dynamics (Eq.~\eqref{eq:bess_soc_dyn}) enforce energy conservation at every time step.

Charge and discharge powers are each limited to the installed C-rate, bounding
the peak power relative to the energy capacity:
\begin{align}
    P_\mathrm{bat,ch}(t)  &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{bat}, \label{eq:bess_ch_lim} \\
    P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t) &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{bat}. \label{eq:bess_dis_lim}
\end{align}
With $r_\mathrm{C} = 0.5\,\mathrm{kW/kWh}$, the battery can be fully charged or
discharged in two hours.

The SoC is bounded between zero and the installed capacity at every hour:
\begin{equation}
    0 \leq E_\mathrm{bat}(t) \leq C_\mathrm{bat},
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:bess_soc_bounds}
\end{equation}
No explicit depth-of-discharge limit is imposed; the full 0--100\,\% usable
window is available to the optimiser.

Simultaneous charging and discharging is not excluded by an additional binary
variable: because both flows incur efficiency losses, operating them
simultaneously is never economically optimal and the solver avoids it
naturally.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Thermal energy storage (TES)}
\label{sec:tes_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Of the TES technologies and modeling approaches reviewed in Sections~\ref{sub:lit_tes_overview} and \ref{sec:comp_modeling_methods}, both sensible heat storage and latent heat storage are retained as competing alternatives, with the optimizer selecting between them endogenously.
The water tank (WTES) is modelled using the stratified 2-zone approach of Section~\ref{sub:sts_model}, with a fixed inner diameter $d = 1.75\,\text{m}$ and a variable height $h_\mathrm{wtes}$ as the sizing variable.
The PCM unit (LTES) uses an isothermal standby-loss formulation calibrated to the Sunamp Thermino\,e product range.

The MILP framework offers two competing TES technologies, between which the
optimiser selects via a binary variable.
Binary variables $y_\mathrm{ltes},\,y_\mathrm{wtes} \in \{0,1\}$ encode this
choice, with the mutual exclusivity constraint:
\begin{equation}
    y_\mathrm{ltes} + y_\mathrm{wtes} = 1.
    \label{eq:tes_select}
\end{equation}
When a technology is not selected its sizing variable is forced to zero by:
\begin{align}
    C_\mathrm{ltes} &\leq C_\mathrm{ltes,max}\,y_\mathrm{ltes}, \label{eq:ltes_select} \\
    h_\mathrm{wtes} &\leq h_\mathrm{wtes,max}\,y_\mathrm{wtes}. \label{eq:wtes_select}
\end{align}
The two technologies are described in the following subsubsections.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Latent thermal energy storage (LTES)}
\label{sub:ltes_model}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The LTES uses a Phase-Change Material (PCM) that stores thermal energy as latent
heat during melting and releases it during solidification, at an essentially
\emph{constant} temperature $T_\mathrm{pcm}$.
Because the driving temperature difference between the tank wall and its
surroundings does not change as the tank charges or discharges, the standby-loss
model differs fundamentally from the SoC-proportional loss of the sensible-heat
WTES: a dedicated isothermal loss model is required.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Governing model equations}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
The thermal capacity is linked to the physical installation volume through the
effective volumetric energy density:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{ltes} = \rho_\mathrm{L,pcm}\,V_\mathrm{ltes},
    \qquad
    \rho_\mathrm{L,pcm} \approx 47.6\;\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}/\text{m}^3.
    \label{eq:ltes_capacity}
\end{equation}
This value is estimated from the four Sunamp Thermino~e models
(Appendix~\ref{appendix:thermino_datasheet}) by dividing each unit's rated thermal
capacity by its outer installation volume (Dim$_1 \times$ Dim$_2 \times$ Dim$_3$),
which yields 38--64\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$/m$^3$ across the range.
Using outer dimensions as the installation footprint is conservative (the PCM core
occupies only part of the enclosure; the remainder is insulation, heat exchanger,
and electronics), so $47.6\,\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}/\text{m}^3$ represents a
lower-bound estimate that avoids over-reliance on thermal storage in the optimiser.

Because the PCM operates isothermally, the hourly standby loss is a linear function
of installed capacity only, with no SoC-dependent term:
\begin{equation}
    \ell_\mathrm{ltes} = a\,y_\mathrm{ltes} + b\,C_\mathrm{ltes},
    \label{eq:ltes_loss}
\end{equation}
where $a$ [kWh/h] is a fixed overhead loss incurred whenever the unit is installed
and $b$ [$\mathrm{h}^{-1}$] is the capacity-proportional loss rate.
The coefficients were obtained by ordinary least-squares regression against
manufacturer standby-loss data from the Sunamp Thermino~e product range
(Appendix~\ref{appendix:thermino_datasheet}), spanning 3.5--14\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$:
\begin{equation}
    a = 0.01667\;\text{kWh/h},
    \qquad
    b = 1.393\times10^{-3}\;\text{h}^{-1},
    \qquad
    R^2 = 0.935.
    \label{eq:ltes_loss_fit}
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:ltes_loss_fit} shows the data and fitted line.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.65\textwidth]{images/ltes_loss_fit.png}
    \caption{Hourly standby loss versus installed capacity for the Sunamp Thermino~e
             product range. Data from the manufacturer datasheet
             (Appendix~\ref{appendix:thermino_datasheet}).
             The linear fit (Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_loss_fit}, $R^2 = 0.935$) decomposes
             total loss into a fixed overhead $a$ and a capacity-proportional
             term $b\,C_\mathrm{ltes}$, reflecting the isothermal operating principle
             of the PCM unit.}
    \label{fig:ltes_loss_fit}
\end{figure}

The stored thermal energy at each hour evolves according to the energy balance:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\mathrm{ltes}(t) = Q_\mathrm{ltes}(t-1)
        - \ell_\mathrm{ltes}\,y_\mathrm{ltes}
        + \eta_\mathrm{ch}\,Q_\mathrm{ltes,in}(t)
        - \frac{Q_\mathrm{ltes,out}(t)}{\eta_\mathrm{dis}},
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:ltes_dyn}
\end{equation}
The loss term $\ell_\mathrm{ltes}\,y_\mathrm{ltes}$ is automatically deactivated
when the LTES is not selected ($y_\mathrm{ltes} = 0$).

The annualised LTES investment cost follows the same linear-affine structure used
for all technologies (Section~\ref{sub:capex_model}):
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{ltes,annual}
        = \alpha_\mathrm{ltes,fixed}\,y_\mathrm{ltes}
        + \alpha_\mathrm{ltes,var}\,C_\mathrm{ltes},
    \label{eq:ltes_capex}
\end{equation}
with $\alpha_\mathrm{ltes,fixed}$ and $\alpha_\mathrm{ltes,var}$ from
Section~\ref{sub:capex_model}.

The high volumetric energy density of the LTES ($\rho_\mathrm{L,pcm} \approx
47.6\,\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}/\text{m}^3$) is roughly \textbf{twice} that of the
WTES ($\rho_\mathrm{w} \approx 22.6\,\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}/\text{m}^3$), so the
shared $V_\mathrm{max}$ constraint gives the LTES access to nearly twice the
thermal capacity in the same footprint.
This is the core economic rationale for including the more expensive LTES: when
available volume is binding, the solver may endogenously select LTES despite its
higher unit cost.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

\begin{itemize}

    \item $y_\mathrm{ltes} \in \{0,1\}$: binary installation decision.
          Required to correctly model the fixed-cost component of the CAPEX
          (Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_capex}) and to deactivate the standby-loss term
          in Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_loss} when the LTES is not selected.

    \item $C_\mathrm{ltes} \in [0,\; C_\mathrm{ltes,max}]$
          [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$]: continuous sizing variable (installed thermal
          capacity), linked to the physical volume by Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_capacity}.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{ltes}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$]: stored thermal energy

    \item $Q_\mathrm{ltes,in}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$/h]: charging heat flow
          at each hour.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{ltes,out}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$/h]: discharging heat
          flow at each hour.

\end{itemize}

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{MILP constraints.}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The technology-selection constraint (already introduced in the TES subsection
intro, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_select}) couples the binary to the sizing variable,
forcing zero capacity when the LTES is not chosen.

Both TES technologies compete for the same physical footprint; the shared volume
limit bounds the maximum installable LTES capacity:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{ltes} \leq \rho_\mathrm{L,pcm}\,V_\mathrm{max}.
    \label{eq:ltes_maxvol}
\end{equation}

The SoC energy balance (Eq.~\eqref{eq:ltes_dyn}) enforces thermal energy
conservation at every hour, incorporating the isothermal standby loss.

Charge and discharge heat flows are bounded by the C-rate limit:
\begin{align}
    Q_\mathrm{ltes,in}(t)  &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{ltes}, \label{eq:ltes_ch} \\
    Q_\mathrm{ltes,out}(t) &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{ltes}. \label{eq:ltes_dis}
\end{align}

The state of charge is bounded between 5\,\% and 95\,\% of full capacity to
prevent complete depletion or overflow:
\begin{equation}
    0.05\,C_\mathrm{ltes} \;\leq\; Q_\mathrm{ltes}(t) \;\leq\; 0.95\,C_\mathrm{ltes},
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:ltes_soc}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Water thermal energy storage (WTES)}
\label{sub:wtes_model}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The WTES is modelled as a vertical, insulated, cylindrical water tank using the
stratified two-zone model described in Section~\ref{sub:sts_model} and
illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:stratifiedtes}.
A fixed inner diameter $d = 1\,\text{m}$ and a variable height $h_\mathrm{wtes}$
are adopted, keeping all capacity-related expressions linear in the sizing variable.
The tank operates between supply temperature $T_\mathrm{H} = 60\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
and return temperature $T_\mathrm{C} = 40\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$
($\Delta T_\mathrm{HC} = 20\,\mathrm{K}$), with a representative indoor ambient
of $T_\mathrm{env} = 10\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Governing model equations.}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The nominal thermal capacity is a linear function of tank height:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{WTES} = \kappa_m \, h_\mathrm{wtes},
    \qquad
    \kappa_m = \frac{\tfrac{\pi d^2}{4}\,\rho_w\,c_w\,\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}}{3.6\times10^6}
    \approx 17.77\;\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}/\text{m},
    \label{eq:wtes_capacity}
\end{equation}
where $\rho_w = 971.8\,\text{kg/m}^3$ (water at $55\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$)
and $c_w = 4{,}190\,\text{J/(kg\,K)}$.

The three-component standby-loss model~\cite{Krien2020oemofsolph} captures the
SoC-dependent lateral wall loss, a baseline capacity-proportional loss, and a
constant lid loss:
\begin{equation}
    \ell_\mathrm{wtes}(t) = \beta\,Q_\mathrm{wtes}(t-1)
                           + \gamma\,C_\mathrm{WTES}
                           + \delta,
    \label{eq:wtes_loss}
\end{equation}
with coefficients derived from first principles.
The lateral wall area of the cylinder is $A_\mathrm{wall} = 4V/d$, giving an
instantaneous wall heat loss:
\begin{equation}
    \dot{Q}_\mathrm{wall} = U_w \cdot \frac{4V}{d} \cdot (T_\mathrm{tank} - T_\mathrm{env}) \cdot \Delta t.
    \label{eq:Qwall_raw}
\end{equation}
In the two-zone model, the bulk temperature depends linearly on SoC,
$T_\mathrm{tank} = T_\mathrm{C} + (Q/C)\,\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}$, so the
driving temperature difference splits into a constant baseline and a
SoC-dependent part:
\begin{equation}
    T_\mathrm{tank} - T_\mathrm{env}
        = \underbrace{(T_\mathrm{C} - T_\mathrm{env})}_{\Delta T_\mathrm{C0}\,=\,30\,\mathrm{K}}
        + \underbrace{\frac{Q}{C}\,\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}}_{\text{SoC-dependent}}.
    \label{eq:dT_split}
\end{equation}
Substituting into Eq.~\eqref{eq:Qwall_raw} and using $C = \rho_w V c_w \Delta T_\mathrm{HC}$:
\begin{align}
    \dot{Q}_\mathrm{wall}
        &= \underbrace{\frac{U_w \cdot 4 \cdot \Delta t}{\rho_w \cdot c_w \cdot d}}_{\displaystyle\beta} \cdot Q
          \;+\;
           \underbrace{\frac{U_w \cdot 4 \cdot \Delta t}{\rho_w \cdot c_w \cdot d} \cdot \frac{\Delta T_\mathrm{C0}}{\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}}}_{\displaystyle\gamma} \cdot C.
    \label{eq:betagamma_derived}
\end{align}
The coefficient $\beta$ is the fractional lateral loss per unit stored energy
(higher SoC means hotter water, larger gradient);
$\gamma = \beta\,\Delta T_\mathrm{C0}/\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}$ is the baseline lateral
loss per unit installed capacity, present even when the tank is empty;
and $\delta$ accounts for the constant top and bottom lid losses.

The SoC energy balance at each hour combines the loss model with charge and
discharge flows:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\mathrm{wtes}(t) = Q_\mathrm{wtes}(t-1)
        - \ell_\mathrm{wtes}(t)\,y_\mathrm{wtes}
        + \eta_\mathrm{ch}\,Q_\mathrm{wtes,in}(t)
        - \frac{Q_\mathrm{wtes,out}(t)}{\eta_\mathrm{dis}},
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:wtes_dyn}
\end{equation}
The factor $y_\mathrm{wtes}$ deactivates the loss term when WTES is not selected.

The annualised WTES investment cost follows the same linear-affine structure used
for all technologies (Section~\ref{sub:capex_model}):
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{wtes,annual}
        = \alpha_\mathrm{wtes,fixed}\,y_\mathrm{wtes}
        + \alpha_\mathrm{wtes,var}\,C_\mathrm{WTES},
    \label{eq:wtes_capex_annual}
\end{equation}
where $y_\mathrm{wtes}$ is the installation binary introduced below.
Without $y_\mathrm{wtes}$, a solver that sets $h_\mathrm{wtes} = 0$ would still
incur the fixed cost term, incorrectly penalising the no-WTES outcome.

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{Fixed parameters and inputs.}
\label{par:wtes_uval}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

All fixed coefficients embedded in the WTES constraints are listed in
Table~\ref{tab:wtes_inputs}.
The wall U-value $U_w$ is the single most influential parameter in the loss model.
Manufacturer datasheets report standby energy consumption [kWh/24\,h] under
EN~12977-3 conditions (tank at $65\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, ambient at
$20\,^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$, $\Delta T = 45\,\mathrm{K}$), from which the implied
$U$-value is back-calculated as:
\begin{equation}
    U_\mathrm{impl} = \frac{Q_\mathrm{standby} \times 1000}{A_\mathrm{tot}\,\Delta T\,24},
    \label{eq:uimpl}
\end{equation}
where $A_\mathrm{tot} = A_\mathrm{lat} + 2A_\mathrm{cross}$ [m$^2$].
Applied to the full Vaillant uniSTOR VPS range
(Appendix~\ref{appendix:vps_datasheet}), the implied values range from
$\approx 0.54$ (VPS~300/3) down to $\approx 0.30\,\text{W/(m}^2\text{K)}$
(VPS~2000/3); Figure~\ref{fig:wtes_validation} shows the results.
The adopted value $U_w = 0.4\,\text{W/(m}^2\text{K)}$ is conservative for the
large-tank range most relevant here, ensuring the optimiser does not over-rely
on thermal storage.

\paragraph{Geometry sensitivity.}
Fixing the tank diameter $d$ is required to keep the WTES capacity variable
($C_\mathrm{WTES} = \kappa\,h_\mathrm{wtes}$, where $\kappa = A_\mathrm{cross}\rho c_p \Delta T_\mathrm{HC}/3.6{\times}10^6$)
linear in the MILP decision variable $h_\mathrm{wtes}$.
However, fixing $d$ without knowing the optimal capacity a priori may yield
unrealistic $h/d$ ratios: a small $d$ forces a very tall, narrow tank to achieve
the same volume, while a large $d$ produces a wide, squat vessel.

To quantify this risk, the full MILP was solved with WTES forced-selected across
eight candidate diameters ($d = 0.75\text{--}3.0\,\text{m}$); results are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:wtes_geom_sens} and summarised below.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Objective insensitivity.}
          The total annual cost varies by only 278\,\euro/yr across the entire
          diameter range (42\,388 vs.\ 42\,110\,\euro/yr, i.e.\ $<$0.7\,\%), confirming
          that the choice of $d$ has negligible economic impact.

    \item \textbf{Capacity insensitivity.}
          The maximum feasible capacity $C_\mathrm{WTES,max} = \rho c_p \Delta T_\mathrm{HC}
          V_\mathrm{max}/3.6{\times}10^6 = 4{,}524\,\text{kWh}_\mathrm{th}$ is
          \emph{independent of $d$} (volume-limited).
          The optimal capacity ranges from 380 to 417\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$
          ($\approx$9\,\% spread), driven only by the slightly different standby-loss
          coefficients $\beta$ and $\gamma$.

    \item \textbf{Geometric realism.}
          The Vaillant uniSTOR VPS range (Appendix~\ref{appendix:vps_datasheet})
          and the Viessmann Vitocell SVPC range (Appendix~\ref{appendix:svpc_datasheet})
          span volumes of 300--2000\,L and exhibit $h/d$ ratios of 2.3--3.7 across
          all models (VPS\,300/3: $h/d = 3.7$; VPS\,2000/3: $h/d = 2.3$;
          SVPC series: $h/d = 1.9$--$2.7$).
          These commercially available tanks therefore suggest $h/d \lesssim 4$ as a
          practical upper bound for a single prefabricated vessel.
          The sensitivity results show that only $d \geq 1.75\,\text{m}$ approaches
          this criterion ($h/d = 4.3$ at the reference choice), while smaller
          diameters yield absurd ratios ($h/d = 50$ at $d = 0.75\,\text{m}$,
          corresponding to a 38-m-tall tank).
          Even at $d = 1.75\,\text{m}$ the optimiser returns $h = 7.6\,\text{m}$,
          which already exceeds the VPS\,2000/3 tank height of 2.4\,m --- reflecting
          that the optimal capacity ($\approx$410\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$, equivalent to
          $\approx$20\,m$^3$) is simply far larger than any single off-the-shelf
          vessel; in practice a battery of tanks would be used.
          Because the objective is robust to $d$ ($<$0.7\,\% spread), the
          fixed-$d$ assumption is acceptable: it does not distort the economics, and
          $d = 1.75\,\text{m}$ is the smallest value that keeps $h/d$ within one
          order of magnitude of the commercial product range.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Decision Variables.}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

\begin{itemize}

    \item $y_\mathrm{wtes} \in \{0,1\}$: binary installation decision.
          Required to correctly model the fixed CAPEX term in
          Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_capex_annual} and to deactivate the standby-loss
          term in Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_dyn} when WTES is not selected.

    \item $h_\mathrm{wtes} \in [0,\; h_\mathrm{wtes,max}]$ [m]: continuous
          sizing variable (tank height).
          All capacity-related quantities follow linearly from this single variable
          via Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_capacity}.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{wtes}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$]: stored thermal energy
          at each hour, governed by Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_dyn}.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{wtes,in}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$/h]: charging heat
          flow at each hour.

    \item $Q_\mathrm{wtes,out}(t) \geq 0$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$/h]: discharging
          heat flow at each hour.

\end{itemize}

% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -
\subsubsection{MILP constraints.}
% - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - - -

The technology-selection constraint (Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_select}, introduced in
the TES subsection intro) couples the binary to the sizing variable, forcing
$h_\mathrm{wtes} = 0$ when WTES is not chosen.

The shared volume limit bounds the maximum installable tank height:
\begin{equation}
    h_\mathrm{wtes} \leq \frac{V_\mathrm{max}}{A_\mathrm{cross}} 
    \label{eq:wtes_hmax}
\end{equation}

The SoC energy balance (Eq.~\eqref{eq:wtes_dyn}) enforces thermal energy
conservation at every hour, incorporating the three-component standby loss.

Charge and discharge heat flows are bounded by the C-rate limit:
\begin{align}
    Q_\mathrm{wtes,in}(t)  &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{WTES}, \label{eq:wtes_ch} \\
    Q_\mathrm{wtes,out}(t) &\leq r_\mathrm{C}\,C_\mathrm{WTES}. \label{eq:wtes_dis}
\end{align}

To prevent the diffuser plates from being submerged at the tank extremes, the
state of charge is bounded between 5\,\% and 95\,\% of full capacity:
\begin{equation}
    0.05\,C_\mathrm{WTES} \;\leq\; Q_\mathrm{wtes}(t) \;\leq\; 0.95\,C_\mathrm{WTES},
    \qquad \forall\,t.
    \label{eq:wtes_soc}
\end{equation}

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Electrical energy balance}
\label{sub:elec_balance}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

At every hour the total power injected into the AC bus must equal the total power
withdrawn:
\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{PV}(t) + P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t) + P_\mathrm{buy}(t)
    \;=\;
    P_\mathrm{dem}^\mathrm{el}(t) + P_\mathrm{bat,ch}(t)
    + P_\mathrm{hp}^\mathrm{el}(t) + P_\mathrm{sell}(t),
    \label{eq:elec_balance}
\end{equation}
where $P_\mathrm{PV}(t) = I_\mathrm{sol}(t)\,C_\mathrm{PV}$ is the PV generation
(Section~\ref{subsec:pv_irradiance}), $P_\mathrm{dem}^\mathrm{el}(t)$ is the
measured hourly building electrical load, and $P_\mathrm{buy}(t),\,P_\mathrm{sell}(t)
\geq 0$ are the grid import and export flows respectively.

To prevent the optimiser from buying grid electricity and immediately re-selling it
at a profit, grid export is capped to on-site generation:
\begin{equation}
    P_\mathrm{sell}(t) \leq P_\mathrm{PV}(t) + P_\mathrm{bat,dis}(t).
    \label{eq:sell_limit}
\end{equation}
This ensures that any exported energy originates exclusively from the PV array or
BESS, not from grid-purchased power.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Thermal energy balance}
\label{sub:therm_balance}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The thermal domain is governed by two coupled equality constraints.
First, all heat output by the HP must be accounted for — it either charges the TES
or is delivered directly to the building heating circuit:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\mathrm{HP}(t) = Q_\mathrm{tes,in}(t) + Q_\mathrm{load,in}(t),
    \label{eq:hp_split}
\end{equation}
where $Q_\mathrm{tes,in}(t) = Q_\mathrm{ltes,in}(t) + Q_\mathrm{wtes,in}(t)$
(exactly one term is non-zero, determined by the TES type selection binaries
$y_\mathrm{ltes}$ and $y_\mathrm{wtes}$) and $Q_\mathrm{load,in}(t) \geq 0$
is the heat bypassing storage.

Second, the hourly heat demand must be fully met by the combination of direct HP
output and TES discharge:
\begin{equation}
    Q_\mathrm{load,in}(t) + Q_\mathrm{tes,out}(t) = Q_\mathrm{dem}(t),
    \label{eq:therm_balance}
\end{equation}
where $Q_\mathrm{tes,out}(t) = Q_\mathrm{ltes,out}(t) + Q_\mathrm{wtes,out}(t)$
is TES discharge and $Q_\mathrm{dem}(t)$ is the BDEW hourly heat demand profile
(Section~\ref{subsec:thermal_demand}).

Together, Eqs.~\eqref{eq:hp_split} and~\eqref{eq:therm_balance} imply that the
HP is the sole thermal source in the system: all heat delivered to the building
originates from HP operation, with the TES acting only as a temporal buffer.
The optimiser can therefore shift HP operation away from expensive peak-price
hours by pre-charging the TES during cheap off-peak periods.

\section{Techno-economic parameters}
\label{sec:econ_params}
% ============================================================

Before presenting the component models it is necessary to establish the economic framework
used to translate one-time capital expenditures into annual costs comparable with the
annual operational expenditures optimised by the MILP.
This section describes the Capital Recovery Factor, the economies-of-scale cost model,
and lists all techno-economic parameters used in the study.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Capital recovery factor (CRF)}
\label{sub:crf}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Investment costs for all technologies are converted to an equivalent \textbf{annualised
CAPEX} using the Capital Recovery Factor (CRF), also known as the annuity factor.
The CRF spreads a one-time investment over the asset's economic lifetime $n$ [yr]
at a fixed discount rate $r$ [-], representing the weighted average cost of capital (WACC):

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CRF}(r,\,n) = \frac{r\,(1+r)^{n}}{(1+r)^{n}-1}.
    \label{eq:crf}
\end{equation}

\noindent A single discount rate of $r = 5\,\%$ is applied uniformly to all technologies,
reflecting a typical WACC for commercial building energy investments in Belgium~\cite{VEKA2026}.
Each technology has its own economic lifetime $n$, so a separate CRF value is computed per
technology.
The annualised CAPEX for a technology with unit investment cost $c$ [\euro/unit] and
installed capacity $X$ [unit] is then:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CAPEX}_\mathrm{ann}(X) = c \cdot \mathrm{CRF}(r,n) \cdot X
    \quad [\text{\euro/yr}].
    \label{eq:capex_ann_linear}
\end{equation}

\noindent A key advantage of the CRF approach is that it enables a fair comparison between technologies with different economic lifetimes.
A PV array with a 25-year lifetime and a battery with a 15-year lifetime cannot be compared on raw investment cost alone: the battery must be replaced sooner and its annualised cost per year is therefore higher relative to its upfront price.
By multiplying each technology's specific cost by its own $\mathrm{CRF}(r, n_k)$, all CAPEX terms are expressed in the same unit (\euro/yr) and can be added directly in the objective function without distorting the trade-off between technologies.

\noindent In practice, the specific cost per unit capacity varies with installation size, which is addressed by the linear-affine CAPEX model described next.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Linear-affine CAPEX model}
\label{sub:capex_model}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Rather than applying a generic economies-of-scale power-law, the capital cost of each
technology is modelled as a \textbf{linear-affine} function of installed capacity,
fitted directly to manufacturer and supplier price quotes (the underlying market data and fitted curves for each technology are presented in Section~\ref{sub:tech_params}):

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CAPEX}(X) = c_f + c_v \cdot X \quad [\text{\euro}],
    \label{eq:capex_affine}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $c_f$ [\euro] is a fixed one-time cost covering installation labour,
mounting, wiring, and commissioning, and $c_v$ [\euro/unit] is the per-unit hardware cost.
The fixed component implicitly captures the qualitative behaviour of economies of scale:
the specific cost $c_f/X + c_v$ decreases as installed capacity grows, without
requiring a non-linear formulation.

This approach has two practical advantages over the power-law alternative.
First, no piecewise-linear approximation of a concave curve is required, eliminating
an additional source of approximation error and reducing solver complexity.
Second, $c_f$ and $c_v$ are directly identifiable from market price data, making the
model straightforward to update when prices change.

Each one-time cost is annualised by multiplying by the CRF:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CAPEX}_\mathrm{ann}(X)
    = \bigl(c_f + c_v \cdot X\bigr) \cdot \mathrm{CRF}(r,\,n)
    = \underbrace{c_f \cdot \mathrm{CRF}}_{c_f^\mathrm{ann}}
    + \underbrace{c_v \cdot \mathrm{CRF}}_{c_v^\mathrm{ann}} \cdot X
    \quad [\text{\euro/yr}],
    \label{eq:capex_ann_affine}
\end{equation}

\noindent which is linear in $X$ and embeds directly as a linear equality constraint
in the MILP.
The fixed annual cost $c_f^\mathrm{ann}$ is gated by the technology-selection binary
$y \in \{0,1\}$ of each component, so it is only incurred when the technology is
actually installed.

variable cost of ${\approx}$\,\euro1.17/L becomes ${\approx}$\,\euro52/kWh$_\mathrm{th}$.
\textbf{This unit conversion is highly sensitive to the assumed temperature swing: halving
$\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}$ to 10\,K would double the specific cost to
${\approx}$\,\euro104/kWh$_\mathrm{th}$, while a wider swing of 40\,K would halve it to
${\approx}$\,\euro26/kWh$_\mathrm{th}$.
The value of 20\,K is specific to the M-accent P\&ID and must be revisited if the
model is applied to a system with a different supply/return temperature regime.}
The resulting specific-cost curves for both TES types are overlaid and compared directly in
Figure~\ref{fig:capex_tes_compare} (Appendix~\ref{appendix:capex_fits}).

% ============================================================
\section{Objective function}
\label{sec:objective_function}
% ============================================================

The MILP minimises the \textbf{total annualised system cost}, which is the sum of
the annualised capital expenditure (CAPEX) for all installed technologies and the
annual operational expenditure (OPEX) including electricity purchase, feed-in
revenue, network tariffs, and a fixed connection cost.
VAT at 21\,\% is applied to all cost components, both CAPEX and OPEX.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Annualised investment cost (CAPEX)}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The linear-affine CAPEX model (Section~\ref{sub:capex_model},
Eq.~\eqref{eq:capex_ann_affine}) is applied to each technology individually.
Each technology $k$ contributes a fixed annual cost $c_{f,k}^\mathrm{ann}$
[\euro/yr], incurred only if the technology is installed, and a variable annual
cost $c_{v,k}^\mathrm{ann}$ [\euro/unit/yr] proportional to its sizing variable.
The fixed component is gated by the corresponding technology-selection binary,
so it is only charged when $y_k = 1$.
The total annualised investment cost entering the objective is therefore:

\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CAPEX}_\mathrm{ann}
    = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{K}}
      \Bigl( c_{f,k}^\mathrm{ann}\,y_k + c_{v,k}^\mathrm{ann}\,X_k \Bigr),
    \label{eq:capex_ann}
\end{equation}

\noindent where $\mathcal{K} = \{\mathrm{PV},\,\mathrm{bat},\,\mathrm{HP},\,\mathrm{ltes},\,\mathrm{wtes}\}$,
$y_k \in \{0,1\}$ is the binary installation decision for technology $k$,
and $X_k$ is its continuous sizing variable
($C_\mathrm{PV}$~[kW$_\mathrm{p}$], $C_\mathrm{bat}$~[kWh], $C_\mathrm{HP}$~[kW$_\mathrm{th}$],
$C_\mathrm{ltes}$~[kWh$_\mathrm{th}$], $C_\mathrm{wtes}$~[kWh$_\mathrm{th}$]).
Annualised coefficients $c_{f,k}^\mathrm{ann} = c_{f,k} \cdot \mathrm{CRF}(r,n_k)$
and $c_{v,k}^\mathrm{ann} = c_{v,k} \cdot \mathrm{CRF}(r,n_k)$ follow directly from
Eq.~\eqref{eq:capex_ann_affine}; their numerical values are listed in
\autoref{tab:econ_params}.

The constraint $y_\mathrm{ltes} + y_\mathrm{wtes} = 1$ ensures exactly one TES
technology is selected, so at optimality only one of the two TES cost terms
is non-zero.
For the WTES, the energy capacity $C_\mathrm{wtes} = \rho_w\,c_{p,w}\,\Delta T_\mathrm{HC}\,
({\pi d^2}/{4})\,h_\mathrm{wtes} / 3.6{\times}10^6$ [kWh$_\mathrm{th}$]
is a linear function of the height decision variable $h_\mathrm{wtes}$, so the
CAPEX term remains linear in the MILP decision variables.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Annual operational cost (OPEX)}
\label{subsec:opex}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Three operational cost terms are summed over the full 8\,760-hour horizon
$\mathcal{T} = \{1,\ldots,8760\}$:

\paragraph{Electricity purchase.}
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{buy} = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \lambda_t^\mathrm{buy}\,P_\mathrm{buy}(t)\,\Delta t,
    \label{eq:opex_buy}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda_t^\mathrm{buy}$ [\euro/kWh] is the hourly all-in consumer purchase
price derived from the ENTSO-E Belgian day-ahead price via the Ecopower dynamic
tariff (Eq.~\eqref{eq:buy_price}, Section~\ref{subsec:price_data}),
stacking the spot component onto fixed per-kWh distribution and levy surcharges,
and $P_\mathrm{buy}(t) \geq 0$ [kW] is the grid import power.

\paragraph{Feed-in revenue.}
Surplus renewable generation can be exported to the grid at the Ecopower
dynamic injection price $\lambda_t^\mathrm{sell}$
($= 0.00098\,\lambda_\mathrm{EPEX}(t) - 0.015$\,\euro/kWh, net of the Fluvius
Imewo injection tariff; Section~\ref{subsec:price_data}):
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{sell} = \sum_{t \in \mathcal{T}} \lambda_t^\mathrm{sell}\,P_\mathrm{sell}(t)\,\Delta t.
    \label{eq:opex_sell}
\end{equation}
This term appears with a negative sign in the objective (it is revenue). The
constraint~\eqref{eq:pv_sellcap} prevents the model from purchasing cheap
grid electricity purely to resell it at a profit.

\paragraph{Capacity (network) tariff.}
Belgian distribution tariffs charge a monthly fee proportional to the highest
15-minute grid-import power recorded in that month~\cite{Fluvius2023}.
Because the model operates at hourly resolution, the 15-minute peak is
approximated by the highest hourly average import power in each month.
This is modelled by tracking a per-month peak variable $\hat{P}_m \geq 0$ [kW]
for each calendar month $m \in \{1,\ldots,12\}$ via the constraints
\begin{equation}
    \hat{P}_m \;\geq\; P_\mathrm{buy}(t), \qquad
    \forall\; t \in \mathcal{T}_m,
    \label{eq:peak_track}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{T}_m$ is the set of hours belonging to month $m$.
The capacity tariff cost is then:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{cap} = c_\mathrm{cap} \cdot \frac{1}{12}\sum_{m=1}^{12} \hat{P}_m,
    \label{eq:opex_cap}
\end{equation}
with $c_\mathrm{cap} = 60\;\text{\euro/kW/yr}$ (Fluvius Imewo published annual rate;
see \autoref{tab:other_econ}).
The average of the twelve monthly peaks $\tfrac{1}{12}\sum_m \hat{P}_m$ is the
\emph{facturatiepiek} (billing peak) on which Fluvius bases the annual charge.
Incorporating $\hat{P}_m$ into the objective incentivises the BESS and TES to
absorb peak demand spikes, thereby reducing the annual network charge.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Complete Objective Function}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

Combining all terms, the MILP minimises:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{
    \min\;\;
    \Bigl(
        \mathrm{CAPEX}_\mathrm{ann}
        + C_\mathrm{buy}
        - C_\mathrm{sell}
        + C_\mathrm{cap}
      \Bigr)\!\cdot\!(1 + \nu_\mathrm{VAT})
}
    \label{eq:objective}
\end{equation}
where $\nu_\mathrm{VAT} = 0.21$ is the Belgian VAT rate applied uniformly to all
cost components (\autoref{tab:other_econ}).
The 21\,\% rate applies because the M-accent building holds a commercial
electricity contract; residential connections qualify for the reduced 6\,\% rate,
which does not apply here (see the Ecopower tariff card in
Appendix~\ref{appendix:tariefkaart}).
All CAPEX figures in \autoref{tab:econ_params} are quoted \emph{excl.\ VAT};
the $1 + \nu_\mathrm{VAT}$ factor in the objective therefore grosses up both
investment and operational costs consistently to the all-in price level faced
by the building operator.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection*{Investment Budget Constraint}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The model includes an optional investment budget constraint on the annualised
CAPEX:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{CAPEX}_\mathrm{ann} \;\leq\; \overline{\mathrm{CAPEX}}_\mathrm{ann}.
    \label{eq:capex_budget}
\end{equation}
Its primary purpose is to enable scenario analysis for \textbf{liquidity-constrained}
situations: by tightening $\overline{\mathrm{CAPEX}}_\mathrm{ann}$, the effect of a
limited investment budget on system sizing and the resulting OPEX can be directly
quantified.
In the standard (unconstrained) case the parameter is set to
$\overline{\mathrm{CAPEX}}_\mathrm{ann} = 250\,000\;\text{\euro/yr}$, which far
exceeds any economically rational solution for the building under study and is
therefore non-binding; it could equivalently be set to $+\infty$ without
affecting the results.

\section{Solver configuration and solution strategy}
\label{sec:solver_settings}
% ============================================================

\subsection*{Solver and Interface}

All optimisation runs are executed with \textbf{Gurobi}~\cite{gurobi}, a
state-of-the-art commercial MILP solver, accessed through the native
\texttt{gurobipy} Python API.
Gurobi is widely regarded as the fastest general-purpose MILP solver available
and is used under an academic licence for this research~\cite{gurobi}.
The model is constructed entirely in Python: decision variables, constraints, and
the objective function are assembled programmatically before being handed off to
Gurobi for solution.

\subsection*{Problem Size}

The full-year formulation with an hourly time resolution covers $T = 8\,760$
time steps.
The resulting MILP contains on the order of $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ decision
variables (continuous and binary) and $\mathcal{O}(10^5)$ linear constraints.
The binary variables arise from the on/off state of the heat pump ($u_\mathrm{hp}(t)$),
the technology-selection decisions ($y_\mathrm{PV}$, $y_\mathrm{bat}$,
$y_\mathrm{HP}$, $y_\mathrm{ltes}$, $y_\mathrm{wtes}$), and the
charge/discharge mutually-exclusive binaries for the BESS.
The large number of binary variables makes the problem computationally intensive,
which motivates the solver settings described below.

\subsection*{MIP Optimality Gap}

Gurobi's branch-and-bound algorithm maintains two bounds throughout the
search: a \textbf{lower bound} obtained by relaxing the integrality
requirements (the LP relaxation), and an \textbf{upper bound} given by the
best integer-feasible solution found so far.
The \textbf{MIP gap} is defined as the relative difference between these two
bounds:
\begin{equation}
    \delta_\mathrm{MIP}
    = \frac{z_\mathrm{UB} - z_\mathrm{LB}}{|z_\mathrm{UB}|},
    \label{eq:mip_gap}
\end{equation}
where $z_\mathrm{UB}$ and $z_\mathrm{LB}$ are the current upper and lower
bound on the objective value.
The solver terminates as soon as $\delta_\mathrm{MIP}$ falls below a
user-specified tolerance \texttt{MIPGap}.
A gap of 0\,\% certifies global optimality; a small positive gap means the
returned solution is within that percentage of the true optimum.
For investment decisions the quality of the solution is not meaningfully
affected by a gap of a few percent, so a non-zero tolerance is set to avoid
the disproportionate computation time required to close the final fraction of
the gap.
The exact tolerance used in each simulation run is reported alongside the
results in Chapter~\ref{chap:results}.

\subsection*{Time Limit}

To keep individual scenario runs tractable, a wall-clock time limit is imposed
via Gurobi's \texttt{TimeLimit} parameter.
If the solver reaches this limit before the MIP gap criterion is met, it
returns the best integer-feasible solution found so far together with the
achieved gap.
The code checks explicitly for this case:
\begin{itemize}
    \item If the time limit is reached \emph{with} at least one feasible
          solution, the run is accepted and the achieved gap is recorded.
    \item If the time limit is reached \emph{without} any feasible solution,
          the run is flagged as infeasible and excluded from analysis.
\end{itemize}
The specific time limit per run is chosen to balance result quality against
the total computation time for the full scenario sweep; the value used for
each simulation is reported in the results.

\subsection*{Hardware Environment}

All computations were performed on a laptop equipped with an
\textbf{Intel Core i7-10750H} processor (6 physical cores, 12 logical
threads at a base clock of 2.60\,GHz) and \textbf{16\,GB} of RAM, running
Windows 11.
Gurobi exploits multi-threading natively: the branch-and-bound tree is
explored in parallel across all available logical threads, which substantially
reduces wall-clock time for the 8\,760-step annual MILP.

\section{Baseline case: current situation}
\label{sec:results_baseline}
% ============================================================

The baseline represents the current situation at the Eelko building: two condensing
gas boilers ($2 \times 80\,\mathrm{kW_{th}} = 160\,\mathrm{kW_{th}}$) covering the
full thermal demand, the existing 34.63\,kW$_\mathrm{p}$ rooftop PV installation
(east-facing, 35\degree{} tilt) supplying part of the electrical load, a
1\,000\,L sensible-water buffer tank, no battery storage, and grid electricity
covering the remainder.
The installation was commissioned approximately ten years prior to this study.

\subsection*{Cost methodology: outsider-viewpoint CAPEX}

Rather than treating the existing assets as fully sunk, the baseline adopts the
\emph{outsider-viewpoint principle}: what would a rational investor pay today to
deliver the same energy services with the same technology?
Sunk costs are irrelevant to this question; only the \emph{undepreciated residual
value} still represents a forward-looking capital commitment.

Capital cost is therefore annualised as
\begin{equation}
  C_{\mathrm{cap},i}^{\mathrm{ann}}
    = S_i \cdot \mathrm{CRF}(r,\, n_i^{\mathrm{rem}}),
  \qquad
  S_i = C_i^{\mathrm{tot}} \cdot \frac{n_i^{\mathrm{rem}}}{n_i^{\mathrm{life}}},
  \label{eq:baseline_capex}
\end{equation}
where $S_i$ is the straight-line salvage value of asset $i$,
$C_i^{\mathrm{tot}}$ its original installed cost,
$n_i^{\mathrm{rem}}$ the years of useful life remaining,
$n_i^{\mathrm{life}}$ the total design lifetime, and
$\mathrm{CRF}(r, n) = r(1+r)^n / [(1+r)^n - 1]$ the capital recovery factor
at discount rate $r = 5\,\%$.
With ten years elapsed out of a 20-year boiler lifetime, for instance, half the
original capital is already depreciated; only the remaining half is annualised
over the next ten years.

\subsection*{Tariff structure}

Electricity prices are based on the day-ahead EPEX spot market for Belgium (2025),
augmented by a buy markup of 8.3\,c\euro{}/kWh and a capacity tariff of
\euro{}5/kW/month applied to the monthly peak grid offtake.
Gas is priced at the TotalEnergies \emph{proEssential Variabel} tariff
(Fluvius Imewo zone, consumption tier 5\,001--150\,000\,kWh/yr), yielding an
all-in variable rate of 5.29\,c\euro{}/kWh\textsubscript{gas} excluding VAT;
the full tariff breakdown is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:tariff_eneco}.
EU ETS\,2 carbon costs are included at \euro{}60/tonne\,CO$_2$, applied at an
emission factor of 0.202\,kg\,CO$_2$/kWh\textsubscript{gas} (natural gas, LHV
basis).
All costs include 21\,\% Belgian VAT.

\subsection{Forward-looking baseline: energy price trajectories}
\label{sub:baseline_forward}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

A static baseline computed at today's prices is a useful reference point but is
insufficient as a decision-making tool.
The economic case for electrification over a 20--25 year asset lifetime depends
critically on how gas and electricity prices evolve over that period, and there is
substantial uncertainty on both sides.

Two structural drivers are expected to increase the relative cost of gas and shift
the baseline upward over time.

The first is \textbf{ETS2}, the European Union's second Emissions Trading System,
which extends carbon pricing to building heating fuels and road transport.
ETS2 is scheduled to enter into force in 2028, with a transitional price cap and
a market-stability reserve mechanism.
The European Commission projects ETS2 carbon prices rising from an initial
cap of around \euro{45}/tCO$_2$ at launch to well above \euro{100}/tCO$_2$ by
the mid-2030s under central projections~\cite{EuropeanCommission2021fitfor55},
though the range across scenarios is wide.
For natural gas (emission factor $\approx 0.202$\,tCO$_2$/MWh$_\mathrm{gas}$)
an ETS2 price of \euro{100}/tCO$_2$ adds approximately \euro{20}/MWh to the
gas commodity price, a cost increase of roughly 40--60\,\% relative to recent
average prices.
This directly raises the operating cost of the gas boiler baseline with each
passing year after 2027.

The second driver is \textbf{electricity price uncertainty}.
While high renewable penetration is expected to structurally lower average wholesale
prices in the long run, short-term volatility remains high and the pace of
decarbonisation affects the spread between peak and off-peak prices.
Lower average prices reduce PV export revenue and the value of BESS arbitrage,
while higher volatility can increase both.

\paragraph{COP multiplier dominates --- and the bar is strongly asymmetric.}
Heat-pump performance is the single most influential parameter.
The tornado bar is markedly skewed: degrading the COP (low end) inflates the
TCO far more than an equivalent improvement reduces it.
This asymmetry is physically intuitive.
The asymmetry is a direct consequence of the convex (hyperbolic) relationship
between electricity consumption and COP: since electricity use scales as
$1/\mathrm{COP}$, equal relative perturbations in opposite directions produce
unequal changes in electricity drawn.
Halving the COP doubles electricity consumption, whereas a 50\,\% improvement
reduces it by only one third.
Even in a hypothetical system with no capital costs, the same 3:1 ratio in
cost impact would arise, demonstrating that the skew is intrinsic to the
physics rather than an artefact of the cost structure.

\paragraph{Electricity price level --- asymmetry driven by optimal re-sizing.}
The price multiplier ranks second, and its bar is also asymmetric, but for a
structurally different reason than the COP bar.
At low electricity prices, PV becomes less profitable relative to simply
buying from the grid, so the optimiser installs less PV.
This produces a double benefit: OPEX falls because grid electricity is cheap,
and CAPEX falls because less investment in PV is warranted.
The two effects reinforce each other, yielding a large TCO reduction.
At high prices the opposite response occurs: PV becomes more attractive and
the optimiser expands it to hedge against expensive grid electricity.
However, this extra PV investment partially offsets the OPEX increase---the
system spends CAPEX to avoid OPEX, so the net TCO rise is smaller than the
net TCO fall at low prices.
The asymmetry therefore reflects the self-limiting nature of the high-price
response (invest to hedge, partial cancellation) versus the self-reinforcing
nature of the low-price response (less investment needed and cheaper to run).

\paragraph{Financial parameters --- large but symmetric.}
The discount rate and PV CAPEX produce bars that are nearly symmetric around
the baseline.
Both act as scaling factors on capital investment: a lower discount rate
(cheaper financing) and a lower PV unit cost equally tilt the investment
decision in favour of larger installations.
Because neither parameter changes the fundamental operating logic of the
system, the cost response is roughly proportional in both directions,
explaining the balanced bar shape.

\paragraph{Technology CAPEX --- mandatory versus optional assets.}
HP CAPEX and battery CAPEX rank next, and their bar shapes reveal an
important distinction.
The HP bar is almost perfectly symmetric: because there is no alternative
technology to deliver the building's thermal load, the optimiser installs
heat-pump capacity regardless of cost, and the CAPEX change passes through
directly to the TCO in both directions.
The battery bar is somewhat smaller and slightly asymmetric toward the
high-cost end, reflecting the fact that battery storage is an optional
component---when it becomes expensive the optimiser simply avoids it, capping
the upside potential of a cost reduction.

\paragraph{Supply temperature --- physically driven, near-symmetric.}
Supply temperature $T_h$ produces a moderately sized, near-symmetric bar.
The relationship is monotonic and physically straightforward: with a fixed
supply-return spread, raising $T_h$ raises the condensing temperature of the
heat pump, which reduces the Lorenz COP and increases electricity consumption
per unit of heat delivered.
Lowering $T_h$ has the opposite effect.
The bar is close to symmetric because the COP--temperature relationship is
approximately linear over the 50--70\,\textdegree C range considered, so
equal temperature steps in both directions produce roughly equal cost
changes.
Although a lower supply temperature also reduces TES energy density, this
effect is secondary since storage CAPEX is a small fraction of total system
cost.
The practical takeaway is that the building's heat-distribution design
(e.g.\ choice between radiators and underfloor heating) directly affects the
economic performance of the integrated energy system.

\paragraph{Low-sensitivity parameters: WTES cost and capacity tariff.}
WTES unit cost and the capacity network tariff sit at the bottom of the chart
with minimal influence on the TCO.
In both cases the physical reason is the same: these parameters govern costs
that are structurally small relative to the dominant terms (HP electricity
consumption and PV investment).
Halving or doubling the WTES unit cost barely moves the total because water
storage is inexpensive per kWh$_\mathrm{th}$ relative to electrical
components.
The capacity tariff enters only through the peak demand charge, which the
baseline dispatch already minimises through HP and TES scheduling.

\paragraph{Decision implications.}
The tornado chart identifies where modelling effort and real-world data
quality matter most.
COP dominates the ranking, which means that the assumed HP performance
curve is the single most important input to validate: an error in COP
propagates more strongly into the TCO than any CAPEX uncertainty.
This motivates using manufacturer-certified or field-measured performance
data rather than generic catalogue values.
Electricity price level and discount rate rank next, both of which are
exogenous market variables outside the designer's control; their large
influence suggests that scenario analysis across plausible price and
financing trajectories is more informative than refining equipment cost
estimates.
PV CAPEX and HP CAPEX, by contrast, are parameters a developer can
actively negotiate, and their moderate ranking confirms that procurement
strategy is a meaningful lever.
Supply temperature is actionable at the building-system design stage:
specifying a low-temperature heat-distribution system (e.g.\ underfloor
heating instead of radiators) directly improves the economic case for the
heat pump.
Finally, the low sensitivity to WTES cost and capacity tariff implies that
decisions about thermal storage technology selection and network tariff
structure are not critical to the overall business case and can be
deferred to a detailed engineering phase.

\subsection{PV CAPEX and electricity price: breakeven map}
\label{sub:sens_pv_breakeven}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The 2D breakeven map is the central result of the sensitivity analysis.
Rather than varying one parameter at a time, it sweeps PV CAPEX and the electricity
price multiplier simultaneously over a grid and records the optimal PV capacity
returned by the MILP at each combination.
The result is partitioned into three qualitative regimes: PV not installed, PV
partially installed, and PV saturated at the 50\,kW$_\mathrm{p}$ roof constraint.
The boundaries between these regimes are the breakeven thresholds.

Figure~\ref{fig:breakeven_dummy} shows an illustrative example of this map generated
with fictive but physically motivated data.
The baseline operating point (532\,\euro/kW$_\mathrm{p}$, price multiplier\,$= 1.0$)
is marked with a star and lies firmly in the saturated region, confirming that at
current conditions the MILP always installs PV at full capacity.
Moving along the x-axis (increasing CAPEX at fixed price) or downward along the y-axis
(falling electricity prices at fixed CAPEX) eventually crosses the saturation threshold,
beyond which partial installation becomes optimal, and then the install threshold,
beyond which PV is not economical at all.

\section{Comparison with traditional rule-of-thumb sizing}
\label{sec:results_traditional}
% ============================================================

Engineering practice has historically relied on simple rules of thumb to size
thermal and electrical storage systems, without solving an optimisation problem.
This section applies those conventional methods to the Eelko building and compares
the resulting system against the MILP optimum, in terms of both total annualised
cost and annual energy performance.
The comparison serves two purposes: it validates that the MILP produces a
meaningfully better solution than naive sizing, and it quantifies the economic value
of optimisation itself.

% ------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Traditional sizing rules}
\label{sub:trad_rules}
% ------------------------------------------------------------

The following industry rules of thumb are applied, each grounded in standard
engineering practice for commercial building HVAC systems.

\paragraph{Heat pump (HP).}
In traditional design the heat pump is sized to cover the design-day peak thermal
load without any storage buffer, giving a conservative, 100\,\% backup capacity.
The measured peak thermal demand at the Eelko building is
120.6\,kW$_\mathrm{th}$ (2021 gas meter data), so the traditional HP size is
$Q_\mathrm{hp,trad} = 120.6\,\mathrm{kW_{th}}$.

\paragraph{Thermal Energy Storage (TES).}
A widely used rule of thumb in energy system modelling sizes TES to cover 6--7
hours of peak thermal load, providing a buffer to allow the heat pump to operate
at a stable part-load point and shift peak demand.
This convention is implemented in the oemof.thermal open-source energy modelling
framework via the parameter \texttt{invest\_relation\_input\_capacity = 1/6},
which couples the storage energy capacity to its rated power in a fixed 6:1 ratio
during investment optimisation~\cite{oemofthermal2024}.
Using the 6-hour rule:
\begin{equation}
    C_\mathrm{TES,trad} = 6 \times Q_\mathrm{hp,trad}
    = 6 \times 120.6 \approx 724\,\mathrm{kWh_{th}}.
    \label{eq:tes_rule}
\end{equation}
The 7-hour rule yields 844\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$.
A more conservative alternative uses the 95th-percentile load
(70.7\,kW$_\mathrm{th}$) rather than the absolute peak, giving
424--495\,kWh$_\mathrm{th}$.

\paragraph{PV array.}
A common rule sizes PV to offset the annual electrical base load, targeting a
self-consumption ratio of approximately 30--40\,\% of annual grid imports.
With the existing 34.63\,kW$_\mathrm{p}$ already installed, a traditional
engineer would typically add no further PV unless a detailed yield study
was commissioned.
Alternatively, PV is sized to fill the available roof area, which gives the
same 50\,kW$_\mathrm{p}$ upper bound used in the MILP.

\paragraph{Battery Energy Storage (BESS).}
A standard rule sizes BESS to store 2--4 hours of average building electrical
load, providing overnight or peak-period autonomy.
The annual mean electrical demand from the Eelko building is approximately
[TODO: insert from meter data]\,kW, giving a traditional BESS size of
